## Why these sources matter
These are the three frameworks most commonly cited by founders when they say "I focus on customers, not competitors." We needed to check whether the frameworks actually support that claim.

## Porter's Five Forces
- Framework IS about competitive structure — asks "is the market structurally winnable?"
- Looks beyond individual competitor actions to: rivalry intensity, new entrants, substitutes, buyer power, supplier power
- Not tracking competitors is a subset of the framework, not an alternative to it
- **Verdict:** Porter requires competitive analysis. "Focus on customers" misreads Porter.

## Christensen's Disruption Theory
- Core thesis: companies are disrupted BECAUSE they focused on existing customers
- Incumbents listened to profitable customers, directed resources toward sustaining innovations
- Missed disruptive low-end innovations that eventually captured the market
- Kodak had digital camera tech but kept serving profitable film customers
- **Critique (Lepore, 2014):** Cherry-picked cases, circular logic. Seagate (Christensen's poster child) grew to $9B.
- **Verdict:** Christensen warns AGAINST pure customer obsession. The Innovator's Dilemma = failing by listening too well to current customers.

## Blue Ocean Strategy
- Subtitle: "Make the Competition Irrelevant"
- BUT: The Strategy Canvas (central diagnostic tool) explicitly maps your offering AGAINST competitors on key factors
- Four Actions Framework (eliminate, reduce, raise, create) requires knowing what competitors offer
- You cannot find a Blue Ocean without charting the Red Ocean first
- **Verdict:** "Make competition irrelevant" is the output. Competitive analysis is the input.
