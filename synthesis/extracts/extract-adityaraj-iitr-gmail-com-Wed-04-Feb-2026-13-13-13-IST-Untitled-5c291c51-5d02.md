# Interview Extract: Tanvir Rajput

**Date:** 4th February, 2026 — 1:13 PM IST
**Role/Company:** Product Marketing Manager (PMM), HackerRank
**Background:** Senior PMM at HackerRank, an enterprise-grade developer assessment platform (~quarterly release cadence). Previously used Klue/Crayon at a prior company (~2022). Currently runs competitive intelligence independently via self-built ChatGPT agents. Primary competitive threat: CodeSignal (fast-moving startup, ~2-week release cycle).

---

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: ChatGPT Already Does What Legacy CI Tools Do

- **Quote:** "They promise to deliver weekly updated battle cards which I can very easily do on ChatGPT also or on Claude. But the only differentiated thing that they promised to do is they design the battle card in a way that I can share it to the customer directly. Like the designing is what something they take care of. But in ChatGPT it's only text based. Such a small design upliftment — I do not think it's worth paying $40,000 to a company."
- **Timestamp:** [09:16]
- **Severity:** Critical — directly challenges the core value prop of any CI platform vs. DIY LLM agents
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** Tanvir has already replicated what he perceives as the primary value of tools like Klue and Crayon using free/cheap ChatGPT agents. He believes the only remaining differentiation is formatting/design — which he doesn't value enough to justify $40K/yr. This is a foundational challenge: if a PMM can self-serve the core output, the platform must deliver something qualitatively beyond weekly digests.
- **Connects to:** ChatGPT/Perplexity comparison objection — consistent with pattern seen across interviews where sophisticated users have built DIY CI workflows

---

### Objection: Competitive Data Goes Stale Too Fast to Justify Investment

- **Quote:** "As soon as you publish a page or as soon as you create a document about a competitor saying that we have this and you don't have this, it becomes stale the next week... which is why mostly competitive positioning is something which is not my P0 typically. We ideally look to get a software to do this and sometimes we do not invest too much into this because of the nature — it gets stale very quickly."
- **Timestamp:** [04:50]
- **Severity:** High — directly undermines willingness to invest in CI tooling at all
- **Category:** Value Prop / Market Fit
- **Summary:** The fast release cycles of younger competitors (CodeSignal ships every 2 weeks) mean any CI artifact is potentially outdated before it's even distributed. Tanvir sees this as a structural reason NOT to prioritize or fund CI tooling. He deprioritizes CI partly because the maintenance burden feels like a losing battle. Kompete's auto-updating claim directly addresses this, but it must be communicated as real-time/continuous — not just "weekly."
- **Connects to:** Stale Data pain point below; connects to ROI justification objection pattern

---

### Objection: CI Is Reactive, Not a Permanent Budget Line

- **Quote:** "Usually comp intel programs come up when there is a direction from leadership to take out competitors on some of your deals... this kind of tool would be useful but not something [that] would only do competitive research."
- **Timestamp:** [12:42]
- **Severity:** High — signals that standalone CI platforms face budget/priority challenges unless embedded in a broader GTM workflow
- **Category:** Market Fit / GTM
- **Summary:** CI investments at HackerRank are triggered by specific executive directives (e.g., a competitive takeout program after losing OpenAI to CoderPad) rather than being a standing budget item. A tool that only does competitive research may not find a home in this buyer's budget unless it plugs into broader ABM, sales enablement, or GTM workflows.
- **Connects to:** New — signals episodic vs. evergreen buying behavior; relevant for Kompete's GTM and packaging strategy

---

### Objection: Battle Cards Have Low Sales Adoption

- **Quote:** "When I have shared these battle cards, there has been low adoption. But usually this kind of interactive material where they can practice their pitch — they just before going on to a conversation — I think this would really help."
- **Timestamp:** [30:55]
- **Severity:** High — the primary deliverable of CI platforms (battle cards) is explicitly called out as low-ROI due to adoption failure
- **Category:** Product / Value Prop
- **Summary:** Tanvir confirms that battle cards — the flagship output of CI tools — sit unused. Sales teams resist static documents and push back on being "taught their job." This creates a gap between CI investment and revenue impact, undermining ROI justification for buyers. He sees interactive pitch-practice as a more adoptable format.
- **Connects to:** ROI justification pattern; connects to sales team resistance dynamic

---

### Objection: Competitor Product Access Is Impossible at Scale

- **Quote:** "For me the problem is to find different ways to get inside their product again and again. Like even if I create a fake account... after two weeks, my free trial period would have got over. And since they have such a short release cycle, they can do it over and over again."
- **Timestamp:** [14:39]
- **Severity:** High — this is framed as the #1 pain, but also an implicit product gap Kompete may not be able to fill
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** Tanvir's single biggest pain is accessing competitor products continuously to verify features and track changes. He acknowledges Kompete (and others) can scrape public surfaces but can't get inside walled product environments. He's skeptical any tool can solve this without user-level access, which scales poorly. This is both a pain point AND an objection to whether any tool can truly deliver on claim verification.
- **Connects to:** New — specific to access/verification challenge; no prior pattern noted

---

### Objection: Competitors Are Lying and You Can't Prove It

- **Quote:** "Sometimes competitors are also lying... they are claiming they have X feature, but inside product there is nothing like that. But in that case, you cannot prove this to your customer who is going through a competitive deal."
- **Timestamp:** [05:33]
- **Severity:** Medium — validates a Kompete differentiator (claim verification) but also highlights how hard it is to solve
- **Category:** Product / Differentiation
- **Summary:** Tanvir describes a specific competitive deal scenario where a competitor falsely advertises a feature. Without product access, he has no way to prove the claim is false to a prospect. This is a compelling use case for Kompete's claim verification feature, but Tanvir's framing also implies skepticism about how any tool could solve this without direct product access.
- **Connects to:** Connects to competitor product access objection above

### Objection: Pricing Opacity — Discovery Only Through Lost Deals

- **Timestamp:** [15:33]
- **Severity:** Medium — validates late discovery pain but also implies pricing intel is lower priority for this persona

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: Comp Intel Programs Are Event-Driven, Not Standing Budget Items

- **Quote:** "Usually comp intel programs come up when there is a direction from leadership to take out competitors on some of your deals. So recently OpenAI was our customer and it got to a competitor called CoderPad and now we are thinking of running a competitive takeout program."
- **Implication for Kompete:** CI tool adoption at mid-market companies may spike around specific competitive events (lost marquee accounts, executive mandates) rather than as a planned annual purchase. Kompete's GTM should include triggers for outreach around competitive news events and should position the platform as "always-on insurance" rather than a project tool.

---

### Insight: Sales Teams Actively Resist PMM-Led Positioning — Prefer Self-Serve Practice

- **Quote:** "When you go and preach something to sales, they literally say don't teach my job to me... We play very safe there. You want to just help them, give them tools to practice, but do not tell them how to talk about this."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Battle card delivery that is prescriptive ("here's what to say") will face organizational resistance. Kompete should frame its sales-facing outputs as practice tools and reference material rather than scripts — and the pitch-practice feature could bypass this resistance entirely by letting reps teach themselves.

---

### Insight: External Agencies Are Being Used as a Workaround for Product Intelligence

- **Quote:** "We are engaging with external agencies to do this for us... They would go to CodeSignal, pretend to be somebody's HR who's looking to buy this software, then engage with their sales team, get a free demo — they do demo on our behalf to understand what is done."
- **Implication for Kompete:** There is a real, budgeted market for competitor product intelligence that companies are currently paying agencies to solve. This is a strong signal that a scalable, automated version of this capability (even partial — e.g., tracking public product pages, free tier feature scraping) could command real willingness-to-pay and displace agency spend.

---

### Insight: The Real Competitive Threat Is the PMM's Own ChatGPT Agent

- **Quote:** "Post AI era what I have done personally — I've created some AI agents on ChatGPT subscription model and it does the same. It scrapes the website of the competitors and it automatically maintains like a live scorecard which gets updated every week and ships inside my email."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The most dangerous competitor for Kompete is not Klue or Crayon — it's the savvy PMM with a $20/month ChatGPT Plus subscription. Kompete must articulate concrete value-add beyond automated scraping and digest generation: historical record, claim verification, team-specific delivery, and cross-signal synthesis are the moats to lean into.

---

### Insight: Fast-Moving Startup Competitors Make CI a Sisyphean Task for Enterprise PMMs

- **Quote:** "HackerRank is on enterprise grade right now and we follow a quarterly release. But a lot of our competitors are still young startups like CodeSignal — they have a release cycle of two weeks. So whatever we launch in a quarter, they would see this is the differentiation and they would release it after two weeks."
- **Implication for Kompete:** For enterprise PMMs competing against agile startups, CI is an asymmetric battle — they can't respond fast enough. Kompete's value in this context is not just tracking changes but helping enterprise teams triage which moves actually matter and providing rapid-response messaging frameworks, not just alerts.

---

### Insight: Competitive Intel ROI Is Measured Qualitatively — No Analytics Exist

- **Quote:** "Nothing that is quantitative. It is usually qualitative insights from AEs or somebody from field saying that this is not working... Only during sales calls we come to know about it."
- **Implication for Kompete:** No buyer in this segment currently has a benchmark for CI tool ROI. This is both a risk (hard to justify spend) and an opportunity (Kompete could define the ROI metric — e.g., win rate in competitive deals, reduction in late discovery incidents). Building analytics that surface these metrics could create a compelling and defensible value narrative.

## Persona Signals

- **Buyer or User?** Both — Tanvir is a likely User (would use the tool daily) and a probable Buyer influencer, but not the ultimate budget owner. Final CI investment decisions appear to come from Director of Demand Gen or VP/CPO level. He would be a strong internal champion.
- **Willingness to pay:** Meaningful price sensitivity. Viewed $40K/yr (Klue/Crayon) as unjustifiable. Found Uplimit compelling at $10–12K but did not purchase due to price. Implicitly comfortable with a tool in the $2–5K range if it delivers tangible, differentiated value beyond what ChatGPT agents can do. Kompete's target pricing is well-positioned for this persona.
- **Decision process:** CI tools are adopted opportunistically — triggered by a competitive event or executive mandate, not through a structured RFP process. Evaluation would be: demo → trial/PoC on a real competitor (e.g., "do this for HackerRank vs. CodeSignal") → qualitative gut check → low-friction procurement. He explicitly invited Aditya to generate a HackerRank-specific report as a next step.
- **Current tools mentioned:** ChatGPT (self-built agent for weekly CI digest), Claude (mentioned as alternative), Outreach, Kaya (internal sales enablement), Uplimit (evaluated, not purchased), Satrs (external competitive research agency), Klue (used ~2022 at prior company), Crayon (used ~2022 at prior company), Gong (mentioned as having a "Compete" feature)
