# Interview Extract: Rajesh Sahu

**Date:** 6th February, 2026
**Role/Company:** Product Manager (Ops-focused) at a large-scale tech company (likely HR/Payroll SaaS based on context); previously worked in an organization with multiple product lines and a layered PM hierarchy
**Background:** Rajesh is a mid-level PM whose primary focus has been reducing operational friction and improving core workflows rather than driving company-level product or pricing strategy. He has some exposure to competitive analysis at the feature/workflow optimization level but has not owned GTM, pricing, or company-wide CI programs.

---

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: Which Layer Are You Solving For?
- **Quote:** "When you say competitive intelligence, which layer are you focusing — like on data collection or maybe insight synthesizing or maybe decision supporting? Which particular layer are you trying to focus?"
- **Timestamp:** [02:37]
- **Severity:** High — directly probes whether Kompete's core value prop is clearly differentiated
- **Category:** Differentiation
- **Summary:** Rajesh immediately asked whether Kompete is a data collection tool, an insight synthesis tool, or a decision support layer. This signals that experienced PMs see these as fundamentally different products. Failing to articulate a clear layer risks being perceived as "just another scraper."
- **Connects to:** New — but relates to the broader ChatGPT/Perplexity comparison objection (anyone can scrape and summarize)

---

### Objection: What Moment Made You Believe This Pain is Real Enough to Pay For?
- **Quote:** "What was that moment that made you feel like this problem is painful enough that teams would actually pay you for that problem to solve?"
- **Timestamp:** [04:34]
- **Severity:** Critical — strikes at whether there is validated demand before product investment
- **Category:** Market Fit
- **Summary:** Rajesh questioned whether Aditya had a concrete "pain moment" that confirmed willingness to pay — not just observed that the problem exists. He was probing for customer evidence, not theory. Aditya admitted they were still exploring and had not yet confirmed WTP, which Rajesh accepted but noted as a significant gap.
- **Connects to:** New — early-stage validation gap

---

### Objection: Summaries Are Commoditized — Anyone Can Build This in a Day
- **Quote:** "Any agent can be built quickly in a day to scrape the data and give the summary. Right. The decision is far more important than any other collection."
- **Timestamp:** [08:31]
- **Severity:** Critical — directly challenges the "AI-powered monitoring" core of Kompete's value prop
- **Category:** Differentiation / Value Prop
- **Summary:** Rajesh explicitly stated that data collection and summary generation are table-stakes, easily replicable with a one-day agent build. He argued that the real value lies in surfacing a *decision*, not a summary. This is a strong commoditization objection against Kompete's foundational layer and positions the strategic intelligence layer as the only defensible differentiator.
- **Connects to:** ChatGPT/Perplexity comparison — "any agent can do this" is a recurring pattern in early CI discovery interviews

---

### Objection: Persona Is Too Broad — You Need to Pick One or Two Primary Users
- **Quote:** "Broader spectrum is always secondary, third role, fourth role. Let's say I can only put finance as a primary role and maybe the other stakeholders are maybe secondary users. I need user segmentation in a strategy — should be on top of the page."
- **Timestamp:** [09:20]
- **Severity:** High — challenges Kompete's multi-persona GTM approach (founder, PM, PMM, Sales, CS)
- **Category:** GTM / Market Fit
- **Summary:** Rajesh pushed back on the idea that competitive intelligence is a pain felt uniformly across CS, Sales, Finance, and Product. He argued for razor-sharp primary persona focus, with everyone else being secondary. Spreading across too many personas weakens positioning, sales messaging, and product prioritization. Kompete's current messaging targets all of them simultaneously.
- **Connects to:** Buyer persona confusion — recurring concern in discovery; relates to who actually owns the budget

---

### Objection: Unclear Usage Frequency — When Would Anyone Actually Open This?
- **Quote:** "How many times are they going to use it? Is it one time? How many times weekly? Is it used in weekly calls or in quarterly calls? When — so all this matters when it comes to product strategy."
- **Timestamp:** [11:12]
- **Severity:** High — if usage is infrequent, pricing and retention become very difficult
- **Category:** Product / Value Prop
- **Summary:** Rajesh raised the concern that competitive intelligence may be low-frequency (reactive, ad-hoc) rather than a daily workflow tool, which would make it harder to justify SaaS pricing or build habit loops. His own company had no structured cadence — competitive reviews happened only when triggered by a feature launch or a competitor move.
- **Connects to:** New — but reinforces the broader concern about whether CI is a "nice to have" vs. a daily workflow

## Pain Points Validated

### Pain: Competitive Data Scattered Across Teams
- **Quote:** "The CS team will have some feedback from the customer. Sales will have some feedback from the people we are trying to sell. Finance will have some feedback on profitability, pricing. Product will have inputs around what the other team probably building, what are we lacking."
- **Severity:** MEDIUM — Rajesh acknowledged the problem exists but wasn't personally the one suffering from it
- **Category:** Tool Fragmentation / No System of Record
- **Currently solving with:** Ad-hoc, cross-functional conversations; no unified system

---

### Pain: Excel Tracking Becomes Stale Quickly
- **Quote:** "Teams don't probably — they have Excel sheets where we used to also track competitors. Pricing, their messaging. We used to create Excels which would become stale after a point. And then you have to create a new one probably."
- **Severity:** HIGH — clearly validated from personal experience
- **Category:** Stale Data / Manual Process
- **Currently solving with:** Excel spreadsheets that are periodically rebuilt from scratch; no live updating

---

### Pain: No Structured Cadence for Competitive Reviews
- **Quote:** "We didn't have a frequency like a certain call. It was like a sudden call schedule and we just entered that call."
- **Timestamp:** [23:51]
- **Severity:** MEDIUM — suggests reactive rather than proactive competitive intelligence
- **Category:** Manual Process / No System of Record
- **Currently solving with:** Ad-hoc meetings triggered by specific competitor events (e.g., a major AI feature launch); no standing competitive review rhythm

---

### Pain: No Notification System for Competitor Content/Launches
- **Quote:** "I would follow them on LinkedIn for new posts, or else I can write a script for their website or blog posts. So whenever they launch a blog, I mean any post, I can have a script in my device which can notify me."
- **Timestamp:** [24:30]
- **Severity:** MEDIUM — Rajesh described manually cobbling together a DIY notification system
- **Category:** Late Discovery / Manual Process
- **Currently solving with:** Manual LinkedIn follows; theoretical personal scripts (not yet built)

---

### Pain: Competitive Intelligence Owned at Senior Level, Not Accessible to ICs
- **Quote:** "Directors used to do it. I mean if you go to any company you'll find all senior directors do them... I've never tracked what was the frequency of that call. It comes naturally, I believe."
- **Timestamp:** [22:55]
- **Severity:** LOW-MEDIUM — signals that CI is siloed at leadership, leaving PMs and ICs underserved
- **Category:** Information Overload / Access to Competitor Product
- **Currently solving with:** Relying on directors/senior leadership to cascade relevant competitive insights down

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: CI Usage Is Reactive and Feature-Scoped, Not Strategic
- **Quote:** "Depends on the problem I'm working in that quarter. If a competitor dropped something literally — they used AI in every place in the onboarding — so we were actually checking their insights, tracking it. We didn't have a frequency. It was like a sudden call."
- **Implication for Kompete:** For mid-level PMs, competitive intelligence is triggered by specific events (competitor launches, backlog prioritization), not a standing daily workflow. Kompete should consider building trigger-based alerts as the activation hook — not positioning as a "dashboard you check daily."

---

### Insight: PMs Value Competitive Intelligence as Workflow Optimization Input, Not Strategy
- **Quote:** "I only scrapped to enhance our core solution. They were doing it in one minute — I optimize for the time, tried to make it 30 or 40 seconds."
- **Implication for Kompete:** At the PM/IC level, competitive intelligence is used tactically to benchmark and optimize specific workflows, not to inform company strategy. Kompete may need two distinct value propositions: one for leadership (strategic CI) and one for PMs (tactical benchmarking and feature gap analysis).

---

### Insight: "Anyone Can Build This" Is a Real Perception Risk as LLM Tooling Matures
- **Quote:** "Any agent can be built quickly in a day to scrape the data and give the summary. Right."
- **Implication for Kompete:** As agentic AI tooling becomes more accessible, technical buyers will increasingly believe they can self-build basic CI pipelines. Kompete must aggressively lead with outcomes (decisions surfaced, deals won, time saved) rather than the mechanics of monitoring and summarization.

---

### Insight: Competitive Intelligence Is Socially Siloed at Senior Levels
- **Quote:** "The hierarchy became so big, so I don't have direct connection with the leadership team. Maybe they're using [a CI tool], I'm not sure."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Even within companies that may already be Kompete customers, ICs and mid-level PMs are often unaware of what CI tools are in use. This is both a distribution problem (CI insights don't flow down) and an opportunity (team-specific delivery as a wedge feature).

## Persona Signals

- **Buyer or User?** User — Rajesh explicitly said pricing strategy and company-level tool decisions were made by founders and senior leadership. He is a potential power user but not a budget owner.
- **Willingness to pay:** Low signal at the individual level. He would build his own script rather than pay for a tool. Budget decisions at his company sit above his level.
- **Decision process:** Rajesh evaluates tools through a rigorous product strategy lens: (1) identify primary user persona, (2) validate pain points, (3) confirm willingness to pay, (4) assess usage frequency. He would be a skeptical but valuable beta tester. For a purchase decision, the champion would need to be a Director, VP of Product, or founder.
- **Current tools mentioned:** Excel (primary competitive tracking), LinkedIn (manual competitor following), personal scripts (aspirational), website/blog manual review, FAQ review
