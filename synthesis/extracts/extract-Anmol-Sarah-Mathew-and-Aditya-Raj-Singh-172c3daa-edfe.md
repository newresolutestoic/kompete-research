# Interview Extract: Anmol Sarah Mathew

**Date:** 25th February, 2026
**Role/Company:** Product Manager / Product Team, Locus (B2B SaaS logistics platform, recently acquired by IKEA)
**Background:** ~4 years in B2B SaaS (currently Locus) and ~2 years in B2C. Previously involved in due diligence reporting for IKEA's acquisition of Locus. Works cross-functionally with GTM, marketing, and pre-sales teams on product roadmap and battle cards.

---

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: Good-to-Have, Not Must-Have (B2B)
- **Quote:** "But then this becomes like a good to have then instead of a must have."
- **Timestamp:** [17:47]
- **Severity:** Critical — directly undermines the core value proposition for the B2B segment
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** Anmol explicitly frames pushed competitive intelligence as a convenience rather than a necessity for mature B2B companies. She acknowledges the value conceptually but cannot place it in the "must-have" category. This positions Kompete against a low willingness-to-pay ceiling for her segment.
- **Connects to:** Likely a recurring objection from mature/post-growth B2B companies; connects to "AOP timing" objection below.

---

### Objection: B2B Competitive Intelligence Use Is Infrequent — Only at AOP
- **Quote:** "The amount of times that I will revisit a competitive intelligence at a B2B space is very limited. Like if you see it will be probably during the AOP time, which is the annual operating plan which you make every year, that's the time and you'll keep revisiting it and then it stops again."
- **Timestamp:** [17:33 – 18:36]
- **Severity:** Critical — low usage frequency = low perceived ROI, severely limits subscription justification
- **Category:** Market Fit
- **Summary:** For a mature B2B SaaS company like Locus, competitive intelligence is a periodic activity tied to annual planning cycles — not a continuous need. This makes it hard to justify an ongoing subscription product. The interviewee explicitly says she would "remember" the product only twice a year.
- **Connects to:** Good-to-Have objection; mirrors a potential pattern across mature B2B companies. New signal for early-stage vs. mature B2B segmentation.

---

### Objection: Word of Mouth From CSMs/TAMs Outperforms Monitoring Tools
- **Quote:** "Even before somebody's automation or somebody's account highlights that this is the product feature that is breaking, making a breakthrough on the market. You will get this information already out there through word of mouth and through CSMs and TAMs in our teams."
- **Timestamp:** [10:13 – 10:30]
- **Severity:** High — suggests existing informal intelligence channels are faster and more trusted than automated tools
- **Category:** Differentiation
- **Summary:** Anmol argues that their customer-facing teams (CSMs, TAMs) already surface competitive product intelligence before any automated system does. This is because B2B clients often use multiple platforms simultaneously and share feedback organically. This directly competes with Kompete's "sub-3-minute alert" and real-time monitoring angle.
- **Connects to:** New — specific to enterprise B2B where client relationships provide a parallel intelligence channel.

---

### Objection: Existing Zapier Automations Already Cover Basic Signal Tracking
- **Quote:** "There are Zapier automations out there which basically gives us the information in terms of where and what we are standing."
- **Timestamp:** [04:02 – 04:40]
- **Severity:** High — incumbency inertia; a low-cost DIY solution is already in place
- **Category:** Competition
- **Summary:** Locus has already built informal automation using Zapier for competitive monitoring. This DIY approach, while imperfect, reduces urgency for a dedicated platform. Switching costs are low but so is motivation to change.
- **Connects to:** Common objection pattern — "we already have something for that." Likely heard across multiple interviews.

---

### Objection: Post-GPT, Consolidating Competitive Info Has Become Easier
- **Quote:** "Post GPT, it has become a little bit easier in terms of cumulating and consolidating that information."
- **Timestamp:** [09:19]
- **Severity:** High — ChatGPT/LLMs are being used as a direct substitute for structured competitive intelligence tools
- **Category:** Competition
- **Summary:** Anmol used GPT herself to build a competitive benchmarking report for IKEA's due diligence process. This signals that LLMs are already eroding the DIY competitive research use case and creating a "free alternative" perception, which directly impacts Kompete's pricing and differentiation story.
- **Connects to:** ChatGPT/Perplexity comparison pattern — a recurring competitive threat in this space.

---

### Objection: B2B Companies Release Very Little Public Information
- **Quote:** "B2B companies will never give you that. Even their website, they release very limited amount of information... So it becomes very difficult for you to figure out like, okay, where do you actually stand?"
- **Timestamp:** [20:45 – 21:28]
- **Severity:** High — limits data quality and coverage for Kompete's core intelligence engine
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** Anmol points out that B2B SaaS companies deliberately suppress public product and feature information, making automated monitoring less effective. She acknowledges her own company's automation also fails to capture this. This is both a validation of the problem and a challenge for Kompete's technical approach.
- **Connects to:** Data validation challenge below; also a structural limitation for the entire competitive intelligence category.

---

### Objection: Validating Competitor Data Is Very Difficult
- **Quote:** "Validating those data is becoming very difficult because you can only like figure it out with LLMs to understand like this might be a potential offering out there, but you will never be sure unless they actually write it down."
- **Timestamp:** [22:10]
- **Severity:** High — raises a trust/accuracy concern about AI-generated competitive insights
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** Anmol explicitly calls out that LLM-inferred intelligence is inherently uncertain for B2B companies that don't publish detailed product info. This is a data integrity objection — users may distrust outputs if they can't be verified, undermining adoption and willingness to pay.
- **Connects to:** B2B information opacity objection; Kompete's "claim verification" feature is directly relevant here.

---

### Objection: Mature/Acquired Company Has a Fixed Roadmap — Less Competitive Urgency
- **Quote:** "Given that IKEA has already acquired us... we kind of have a roadmap built in. We know exactly because of a client base what we need to build to get the money in."
- **Timestamp:** [18:36 – 19:16]
- **Severity:** Medium — specific to Anmol's company stage, but signals a broader ICP consideration
- **Category:** Market Fit
- **Summary:** Post-acquisition or post-PMF companies with a stable client base and clear roadmap feel less urgency to monitor competitors. They're building to satisfy existing clients, not chasing market position. This suggests Kompete's ICP should skew toward earlier-stage or actively-competing companies.
- **Connects to:** New — important ICP segmentation signal.

---

### Objection: Pricing Concern Raised Immediately Upon Seeing Product
- **Quote:** "It's very good to have. It's good to have information. But what is the pricing for this entire thing?"
- **Timestamp:** [28:44]
- **Severity:** Medium — pricing sensitivity is high, but she left before hearing an answer
- **Category:** Pricing
- **Summary:** Anmol's first reaction after the product demo was to ask about pricing, and she had to leave before receiving an answer. Given her "good to have" framing throughout, this suggests she is evaluating whether the value justifies any paid tier at all. ROI justification will be critical in any follow-up.
- **Connects to:** ROI justification pattern; pricing sensitivity common across B2B buyers.

## Pain Points Validated

- **Quote:** "The competitor information or intelligence basically resides with different teams and there's no one common platform."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** Tool Fragmentation
- **Currently solving with:** Each team manages independently — product marketing tracks signals, CEO receives a curated report, product team reviews reports periodically. No shared system.

---

### Pain: High Manual Effort to Gather B2B Competitive Intelligence

- **Severity:** HIGH (especially for B2C; MEDIUM for mature B2B)
- **Category:** Manual Process
- **Currently solving with:** Zapier automations + product marketing team manual research + word of mouth from CSMs/TAMs.

---

### Pain: B2B Competitor Pricing Is Opaque and Hard to Obtain

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Category:** Pricing Opacity
- **Currently solving with:** Suspected manual first-person research (cold outreach, trial signups). Exact method unclear even to the interviewee.

---

### Pain: B2B Competitor Product Features Are Difficult to Track

- **Category:** Access to Competitor Product
- **Currently solving with:** Product blogs, release notes, client word-of-mouth via CSMs/TAMs.

---

### Pain: Battle Cards Require Manual Verification and Update

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: B2B Competitive Intelligence Need Is Lifecycle-Stage Dependent
- **Quote:** "If you would have asked me this question four years back, I would have said it was much more [important] because we're still competing. Now given that IKEA has already acquired us... we kind of have a roadmap built in."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Kompete's ICP should explicitly target B2B SaaS companies in active competitive growth phases ($5M–$100M ARR, pre-acquisition, not yet dominant in segment). Post-PMF or acquired companies are a poor fit and will consistently give "good to have" feedback.

---

### Insight: B2B Buyers Define "Competitor" Situationally Based on Client Being Pitched
- **Quote:** "What defines a competitor for a B2B changes from the client that they are pitching to... Nobody wants to say that A and B is a competitor. They all want to say that my product is an entirely different product. There's no one in the market for that."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Kompete should support dynamic competitor sets configurable by deal/segment/client type, not just static competitor lists. The Strategy Timeline and battle cards should allow context-switching by audience.

---

### Insight: B2C Is a Vastly Higher-Urgency Market for Competitive Intelligence
- **Quote:** "For B2C, it's like a bread and butter — like you'll always be looking at the competitor first before you do anything. Even for a retail company, anybody would love that information to be always there on top of their code before they can do anything else."
- **Implication for Kompete:** If the current B2B SaaS positioning consistently yields "good to have" feedback, Kompete should seriously evaluate B2C-focused companies or B2B companies serving consumer markets as a wedge segment where urgency and willingness to pay are materially higher.

---

### Insight: CRM/CSM-Sourced Intelligence Is the Dominant Channel for Mature B2B — Not Monitoring
- **Quote:** "We rely more in terms of the word of mouth from our clients itself who are using that particular platform or have used it."
- **Implication for Kompete:** A CRM/CSM integration layer (similar to what Klue does) could be a powerful differentiator for Kompete among mature B2B companies. Without it, Kompete's value to this segment remains "nice to know" rather than "operationally essential."

---

### Insight: The AOP Cycle Is the Natural Buying Moment for B2B Competitive Intelligence
- **Quote:** "It will be the when will you remember about that particular product, or when will be the need for that particular product that will arise? It's always like maybe once in half year... always be remembered during the AOP time period."
- **Implication for Kompete:** GTM timing should target outreach 6–8 weeks before typical AOP cycles (Q3/Q4 for most companies). Messaging should frame Kompete as the system that makes AOP competitive analysis 10x faster — anchoring on a known, recurring need.

---

## Persona Signals

- **Buyer or User?** User — Anmol is a product manager who consumes and contributes to competitive intelligence but does not control budget. She explicitly said the pricing function and budget decisions sit elsewhere (CEO, marketing team, pre-sales).
- **Willingness to pay:** Low personal advocacy signal. Consistently framed the product as "good to have" and her first instinct after the demo was to ask about pricing rather than express intent. Would likely not champion internally without a stronger ROI narrative.
- **Decision process:** Her company would likely evaluate during AOP planning. She suggested routing specific questions to the pre-sales team. Decision involves CEO visibility (CEO receives competitive reports). Trial/freemium model suggested as necessary precondition to purchase.
- **Current tools mentioned:** Zapier (automations for competitive signals), ChatGPT/GPT (ad hoc competitive analysis and due diligence), word of mouth via CSMs and TAMs, internal marketing reports, product blogs and release notes.
