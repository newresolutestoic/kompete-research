# Interview Extract: Rebin J Anselm

**Date:** 3rd February, 2026
**Role/Company:** Product Manager at Quest Global (handling Hamara Benefits — an employee benefits platform for blue-collar workers); previously Product Marketing Manager
**Background:** 7+ years of SaaS product building experience across product management and product marketing roles. Currently owns product roadmap for an employee benefits/rewards platform serving 6–6.5 lakh blue-collar employees in India.

---

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: Automation Can't Replace Human Visual/Positioning Analysis
- **Quote:** "For competition analysis, for a product marketing perspective, I think it should be done, you know, manually to some extent at least. And they have to look at the website, how they are presenting the visual kind of approach that they are bringing in. Right? So that I think it...should not be automated, at least that part should not be automated."
- **Timestamp:** [27:33]
- **Severity:** High — directly challenges the scope of automation Kompete promises
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** Rebin believes that capturing the "look and feel" of a competitor's website, their visual design language, and how they articulate use cases requires human judgment. He's skeptical that AI can meaningfully replicate this. He accepts AI for data aggregation and market research but draws a firm line at qualitative brand/UX analysis.
- **Connects to:** Likely connects to the broader "ChatGPT/Perplexity comparison" objection pattern — if AI tools already do the quantitative parts, the remaining value must be in qualitative insight, which he says needs to stay human.

---

### Objection: Existing AI Tools Already Cover Basic Competitive Research
- **Quote:** "This has become very easier nowadays and with the type of record Rails and stuff, we can build it...I know Gemini and ChatGPT, right. So it gave me like what are all the kind of industries that are around these offerings."
- **Timestamp:** [05:24] / [03:04]
- **Severity:** High — positions free tools as a viable substitute for product discovery and basic mapping
- **Category:** Differentiation
- **Summary:** Rebin uses ChatGPT and Gemini to map competitive landscapes and identify competitor categories. He also references Google's AI mode (Deep Research) as equally useful. This creates a substitution risk where the "research" portion of Kompete's value prop is seen as already solved by general-purpose AI.
- **Connects to:** ChatGPT/Perplexity comparison pattern — recurring signal across interviews.

---

### Objection: Product Engagement / Silence Risk
- **Quote:** "If a product is silent for the rest of the month, somebody subscribing for Anvil and if the product is not, you know, it's not very, you know, the user is not interacted, they'll think of using it in the next month and the next year. So this silence should not be there."
- **Timestamp:** [30:34]
- **Severity:** Medium — this is a retention/engagement risk framed as a product design challenge
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** Rebin flags that competitive intelligence tools can easily go dormant between use cycles. If the product doesn't proactively push value to the user, churn becomes likely. He is essentially articulating the problem that Kompete's own alert/digest model needs to solve — but framing it as an unsolved challenge he's skeptical about.
- **Connects to:** New — product engagement / push vs. pull design concern. Relevant to Kompete's sub-3-minute alert and weekly digest features.

---

### Objection: Competitive Intel Broadened to "Stakeholder Intelligence" — Scope Confusion
- **Quote:** "I used to think it in a way that it is a stakeholder intelligence platform. So the intelligence is like we are trying to take a pulse of somebody, a dipstick of somebody in the market. So it can be a customer, it can be a partner, it can be an investor, it can be a competitor as well."
- **Timestamp:** [06:14]
- **Severity:** Medium — not a rejection, but signals that the "competitive intelligence" label may be too narrow for how sophisticated PMs think about the problem space
- **Category:** Positioning / Market Fit
- **Summary:** Rebin reframes competitive intelligence as a subset of "stakeholder intelligence," which includes customers, partners, employees, and investors. This could mean Kompete is under-selling its scope or that the positioning needs to be broader to resonate with senior PMs who think systemically.
- **Connects to:** Buyer persona confusion — PMs may have a different mental model of the problem than what Kompete's current messaging addresses.

---

### Objection: Pricing Data in B2B Is Inherently Hidden — Tool Can Only Go So Far
- **Quote:** "In B2B space the challenge is we don't get the pricing details outside...they'll put like 2013 on the website. But you know, if we are negotiating, if we are actually looking at it would be much lesser than that."
- **Timestamp:** [20:24] / [23:40]
- **Severity:** Medium — this is a structural limitation of the market, not of Kompete specifically, but it does cap the value of pricing intelligence features
- **Category:** Product / Value Prop
- **Summary:** Rebin highlights that B2B pricing is opaque by design — list prices are rarely real prices. He acknowledges that even if Kompete scrapes pricing, the negotiated price won't be visible. He suggests that prospect conversations are actually the best source of real pricing data, which points toward a gap in Kompete's current intelligence model.
- **Connects to:** Pricing Opacity — similar pattern likely to appear across interviews with B2B-focused PMs.

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: Competitive Intelligence Frequency Is Role-Dependent — PMs vs. PMMs Have Very Different Cadences
- **Quote:** "From product management perspective, it is required at least once or twice in a quarter. But for product marketing perspective, what I would see is probably once in a...twice in a year."
- **Timestamp:** [29:35]
- **Implication for Kompete:** GTM messaging and pricing should be segmented by role — PMs are higher-frequency, higher-urgency buyers; PMMs are periodic, strategic users. Consider two distinct ICPs with different activation hooks and value narratives.

---

### Insight: Competitive Intelligence Intensity Is Proportional to Whether You Are "In the League"
- **Quote:** "If the industry is competitive and we are in the league of competition...in that case competitive intelligence is much more needed. For product management perspective, it is required at least once or twice in a quarter. But in case of Hamara Benefits, the recent product which I'm working on, I'm not even in the league."
- **Timestamp:** [28:37]
- **Implication for Kompete:** Target companies that are actively competing for market share, not nascent or niche players. ICP filtering criterion: is the company "in the league" — i.e., tracking head-to-head with named competitors? If not, activation will be low.

---

### Insight: Third-Party Comparison Sites Are a More Trusted Pricing Source Than Vendor Websites
- **Quote:** "It is not from their company's website, it is from a comparison website which is done by a different company, a very small startup. They did a comparison between Advantage Club and their company there. They are mentioning this pricing."
- **Timestamp:** [05:24]
- **Implication for Kompete:** Kompete's pricing intelligence should explicitly index comparison sites (G2, Capterra, niche comparison startups) as primary sources — and surface this as a differentiator over manual Googling or scraping vendor sites directly.

---

### Insight: Prospects Are an Untapped Source of Real Competitor Pricing Data
- **Quote:** "Prospects. They would have already done a lot of research about our market, about our competition. Right. If you're asking a question, what was your, you know, what are the companies that you have checked? Vantage circle. This is. What is the pricing for Vantage Circle they have quoted."
- **Timestamp:** [24:36]
- **Implication for Kompete:** There's a product idea here around prospect-side intelligence (integrating with CRM or sales call data) that no current competitor is doing at scale. This could be a moat-building differentiator if Kompete captures inbound prospect signals about competitor mentions.

---

### Insight: Interviewee Is Building an Adjacent Competitive/Customer Intelligence Product
- **Quote:** "I recently am working with some of my friends in building Microsoft's tool which is, you know, like a side project. So it is more into customer intelligence platform."
- **Timestamp:** [06:14]
- **Implication for Kompete:** Rebin is a potential indirect competitor (early stage, India-focused). His interest in Kompete's beta is genuine but carries a dual motive — he may be evaluating the product as a reference point for his own build. Monitor relationship carefully; could be partner or future competitive signal.

---

### Insight: "Proactive vs. Reactive" Intelligence Framing Is a Strong Mental Model for PMs
- **Quote:** "Instead of being reactive is the chatbot being reactive? We are typing, asking a question, it is reacting to our questions. So this is a proactive approach. It will automatically trigger at certain events."
- **Timestamp:** [08:07]
- **Implication for Kompete:** The proactive/reactive framing resonates deeply with PMs who are aware of the limitations of query-based research. Kompete's marketing should lean into "proactive intelligence" language as a contrast to ChatGPT/Google — you don't ask, it tells you.
