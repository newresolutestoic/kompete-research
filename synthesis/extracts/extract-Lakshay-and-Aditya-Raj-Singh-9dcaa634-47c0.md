# Interview Extract: Lakshay Bhatarah

**Date:** 8th February, 2026
**Role/Company:** Pricing & Pre-Sales Specialist, Genpact (Financial Services Vertical — Banks, Insurance, Fintech); previously at an unnamed BPO firm and briefly at KPMG (Financial Due Diligence, 10 months)
**Background:** ~8–10 years in pricing and finance across large BPO/services firms. Lakshay builds pricing models for complex multi-year outsourcing deals in financial services. He consumes competitive intelligence (via Everest Group, BCG, McKinsey reports) but does not build SaaS products or price them — making him an adjacent, not direct, persona for Kompete.

---

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: Enterprise Pricing Is Opaque by Design
- **Quote:** "I would say it will be very difficult to have it because it's very personal to them. I haven't seen any such... the problem that you have to collate everything, even the pricing I think it would be very difficult because that information is not readily available."
- **Timestamp:** [16:18]
- **Severity:** High — directly challenges the core data-collection viability of the platform
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** Lakshay argues that enterprise/sales-led SaaS pricing is inherently confidential and negotiated 1:1 per client. Publicly available list prices bear little resemblance to actual transaction prices. He has personally witnessed a 25% discount gap between what Freshworks charged Genpact versus the same client directly. He doubts any platform can reliably surface true negotiated prices.
- **Connects to:** New — pricing opacity is a known assumption at Kompete, but Lakshay's specificity (naming the Freshworks example, the 25% gap) gives it more weight than abstract skepticism.

---

### Objection: Competitive Benchmarking Data Is Frequently Inaccurate
- **Quote:** "I for once we know that I have worked in two different companies. I know the price points they provide for certain locations are not at all correct. Nobody can match that or sell those at price point... That's why we have kept it as a third benchmark in our whole process."
- **Timestamp:** [59:22]
- **Severity:** Critical — if even paid reports (Everest, millions of dollars) are inaccurate, a cheaper automated platform faces even greater credibility risk
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** Lakshay explicitly states that Everest Group's benchmarking reports — which cost millions — contain price points that do not reflect reality. He and colleagues from multiple companies have independently confirmed this. Despite the inaccuracy, companies still pay for these reports and rely on them as a "third benchmark." This raises a critical question: will Kompete's data be more accurate, and how will it prove that?
- **Connects to:** New — but directly undermines the "democratizing intelligence that was previously only available to KPMG/BCG clients" angle if the underlying data sourcing problem is unresolved.

---

### Objection: Buyer vs. Seller Persona Confusion
- **Quote:** "So right now you're, I'm just trying to understand you're preparing a competitor benchmarking environment for companies. Like if they want some information like how much this product would sell for in the market, how much should they pitch?"
- **Timestamp:** [08:41] and clarification at [13:07]
- **Severity:** Medium — Lakshay initially assumed the product was for end-buyers (procurement teams), not for the internal product/marketing/pricing teams of SaaS companies. Required explicit correction by Aditya.
- **Category:** GTM
- **Summary:** Lakshay repeatedly misread the target persona as a buyer of SaaS software rather than the seller's internal pricing/marketing/product team. This confusion required multiple clarifying exchanges and persisted until nearly the midpoint of the call. Positioning language is not self-evident to an adjacent but non-target persona.
- **Connects to:** New — but signals a GTM positioning risk for any outbound/cold outreach to senior enterprise professionals who may not immediately recognize themselves as the buyer.

---

### Objection: Sourcing Private Pricing Data Raises Legal/Ethical Concerns
- **Quote:** "I think nobody would want that... I think the only the people who actually have a view of pricing... it would be a breach of contract for us."
- **Timestamp:** [51:52] and [52:18]
- **Severity:** High — raises questions about data provenance and whether target users would contribute or trust data sourced this way
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** When Aditya floated the idea of a crowdsourced pricing platform where contributors are paid, Lakshay immediately flagged that sharing internal pricing information would constitute a breach of contract for pricing and sales team employees. He acknowledged that Everest likely sources data through similar back-channels but that doesn't make it acceptable. This creates a trust/compliance barrier for user-generated data contributions.
- **Connects to:** New — directly relevant to any crowdsourced or user-contributed data model Kompete might consider.

- **Timestamp:** [14:00] and [44:42]

## Pain Points Validated

- **Quote:** "From my experience on the pricing this is the most problematic area of our whole process where we don't know what the other vendors are pricing and how they are pricing and pitching it to the end clients."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** Pricing Opacity
- **Currently solving with:** Everest Group reports (millions of dollars, infrequent, acknowledged as inaccurate), BCG/McKinsey tech benchmarks, anecdotal intel from sales team (described as "99% of the time is just a random number that they want")

---

- **Severity:** HIGH

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: The Everest Group Model Is the Incumbent — and It's Broken
- **Quote:** "They very clearly say that this is something that we feel are the rates in the market. They're not giving any guarantee for it, but they take millions of dollars for it still... I know the price points they provide for certain locations are not at all correct. Nobody can match that or sell those at price points."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The dominant competitive intelligence product in enterprise pricing (Everest, BCG, McKinsey reports) is expensive, infrequent, self-admittedly unreliable, and delivered in static Excel/PDF format. Kompete doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be more accurate, more current, and a fraction of the cost to displace a deeply entrenched but legitimately flawed incumbent.

---

### Insight: Win Rate Collapse Is the Crisis That Creates Budget
- **Quote:** "In the last one year Irwin ratio which was around 65% got to 5% which was like a lot of heads shifted. A lot of firing happened because of that. So process got added in our pricing that you have to do competitor analysis."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Competitive intelligence adoption is triggered by acute business pain — specifically, losing deals at an alarming rate. Sales and pricing leaders who have experienced a win-rate collapse have budget authorization and urgency. GTM messaging should lead with win rate recovery, not generic "know your competitors" framing.

---

### Insight: Agentic AI Pricing Is an Uncharted Territory — and Everyone Knows It
- **Quote:** "As of now it's a very new market for everyone. Even Accenture TCS because nobody knows how to price for it but they are pitching it to the client. So something is going to them. But the problem is that nobody's able to understand how that will happen... Everest has been asked to give us a report. I don't know how they will do it."
- **Implication for Kompete:** A vertical opportunity exists specifically around monitoring how competitors are pricing and positioning AI/agentic products. This is a blind spot even for Everest Group. A Kompete feature tracking competitor AI pricing evolution (token-based vs. subscription vs. transaction vs. gain-share) could be a powerful category-defining capability that no incumbent offers.

---

### Insight: Value/Gain-Share Pricing Is Emerging as the New Battleground
- **Quote:** "Gain share will be the next thing. Like clients are usually not comfortable now something upfront they're saying that you give us the savings and you take something... Accenture has already agreed to take deals from us and starting buying preferential shares of the clients."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The shift from per-seat/license to value-based and gain-share pricing models is accelerating. Competitors who first adopt these models will gain major market advantage. Kompete's competitive tracking should explicitly monitor pricing model changes (not just price levels) as a strategic signal — this is a form of "positioning drift" that Kompete's Strategy Timeline feature is well-suited to capture.

---

### Insight: Two-Sided Market — Both Buyers and Sellers Are Currently Paying Millions for the Same Data
- **Quote:** "Every contract has that claw bench that if a client finds a similar service in the market at a lesser price or the same, they can compel their existing vendor to match it... those clients pay the same amount of money to Everest BCGs that we are paying."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The competitive intelligence market is currently two-sided — enterprise buyers pay consultants to benchmark what vendors are charging, and vendors pay the same consultants to benchmark what competitors are charging. A platform that serves either side (starting with vendors/SaaS companies) could expand to procurement teams as a second buyer segment, dramatically increasing TAM.

---

### Insight: Messaging Drift Is Already Recognized as Strategically Important — Even Without a Tool for It
- **Quote:** "They have changed the messaging from autonomous finance to AI agent positioning. So these kind of things... what is your competitor doing in the month of say Jan... so there's no timeline for us to also understand their transition."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Aditya's own observation (supported by Lakshay's nodding agreement) that competitors shift messaging without anyone tracking the historical record validates Kompete's "Strategy Timeline" differentiator. The framing "they changed from X to Y and you missed it" is a resonant, concrete use case to lead with in sales conversations.

## Persona Signals

- **Buyer or User?** Neither — Lakshay is an **adjacent informant**, not a direct buyer or user of Kompete. He prices BPO/outsourcing services, not SaaS products, and his competitive intelligence comes from Everest Group/BCG/McKinsey reports. He validated the pain and pointed toward the right buyer (SaaS product pricing teams, startup pricing leads, Salesforce/Google monetization teams).
- **Willingness to pay:** Not directly applicable. However, his organization already pays millions to Everest/BCG for inaccurate benchmarking data, suggesting that the enterprise pricing budget for competitive intelligence is real and substantial. He expressed enthusiasm for a platform that democratizes access to this data.
- **Decision process:** In his current role, tools/reports are approved through finance/pre-sales leadership. He described needing to justify pricing recommendations against three benchmarks (recent internal deals, existing running deals, Everest external benchmark). A Kompete report would likely slot in as the "external benchmark" replacement — but would need to be credible enough to defend in deal reviews.
- **Current tools mentioned:** Everest Group (benchmarking reports, millions/yr), BCG (tech rate cards), McKinsey (pricing benchmarks), Excel (internal pricing models), APN (banking automation tool used with clients), Amazon Connect (license), Microsoft Azure (Genpact's agentic AI backend), Freshworks (named as a competitor who undercut them on pricing)
