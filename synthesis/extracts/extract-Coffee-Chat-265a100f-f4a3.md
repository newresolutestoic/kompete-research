# Interview Extract: Shobit Bhargava

**Date:** 3rd February, 2026
**Role/Company:** Head of Sales, Partnerships & Pricing Strategy at a ~30-member B2B SaaS startup (Bangalore); transitioning to Fractional CRO & Pricing Consultant
**Background:** Shobit has experience across large enterprises and startups, primarily in the consumer and tech industry. At his current company (~150+ clients), he owns pricing strategy, sales, and partnerships, and is beginning to take on fractional advisory roles at other startups.

---

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: B2B Pricing Signals Are Unreliable by Nature
- **Quote:** "B2B pricing is always variable and dynamic. Depending on the client's requirement, depending on the client's budget, depending on the client's stature and depending on the sales guy pitching it... signals I'm not sure because we also did something recently. But absolutely useless."
- **Timestamp:** [11:46] – [13:01]
- **Severity:** Critical — directly challenges the pricing intelligence pillar of the product
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** Shobit argues that B2B pricing is inherently negotiated, contextual, and salesperson-dependent. Any signal captured from public sources would be a list price, not the actual transactional price. He experimented with something similar and dismissed the result as "absolutely useless," suggesting this isn't theoretical skepticism but lived experience.
- **Connects to:** New — but highly likely to recur with any B2B pricing persona; aligns with the broader "contact sales" pricing opacity problem Aditya himself acknowledged

---

### Objection: Startups Won't Pay for Competitive Intelligence
- **Quote:** "Startups tend to believe that they their product is the best and this is the niche and they don't look at those smaller things so minutely and they wouldn't spend money to get something like this. Mid market and beyond."
- **Timestamp:** [27:32]
- **Severity:** High — directly challenges GTM targeting if Kompete is going downmarket
- **Category:** GTM / Market Fit
- **Summary:** Shobit believes early-stage startups are overconfident about their positioning and won't invest in competitive monitoring. He explicitly places the viable buyer segment at mid-market and above. This narrows the addressable TAM at the low end significantly.
- **Connects to:** New — but consistent with the $5–200M ARR target segment framing in Kompete's own positioning

---

### Objection: After Initial Benchmarking, Ongoing CI Has Low ROI
- **Quote:** "I don't think so. We look we keep researching on competition pricing on a regular basis... After I think the first six months we did a competition benchmarking went to about six, seven competitors... broadly benchmarks set so we don't keep changing the price and we don't even put too much effort looking at competition pricing."
- **Timestamp:** [15:01] – [16:07]
- **Severity:** High — undermines the "continuous monitoring" and real-time alert use cases
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** Shobit describes CI as a one-time or infrequent exercise rather than an ongoing workflow. Once benchmarks are set, there is minimal motivation to revisit them unless a major disruption occurs (e.g., a competitor completely changing their pricing model). This challenges Kompete's 24/7 monitoring and sub-3-minute alert value proposition.
- **Connects to:** New — though likely a recurring theme in interviews with small/mid-size companies that don't have dedicated strategy functions

---

### Objection: Their Product Is Too Simple to Warrant Deep Competitive Monitoring
- **Quote:** "Maybe our product is not so complex. That's why we are not going to G2s and checking what all the pain points are. Simple product. So we don't really look too much at competition."
- **Timestamp:** [18:15]
- **Severity:** Medium — speaks to segment fit, not a universal objection
- **Category:** Market Fit
- **Summary:** Shobit explicitly says that product simplicity reduces the motivation to do feature-level competitive analysis or G2 review monitoring. The implication is that complex, multi-feature B2B SaaS products are far more natural buyers than simple, single-use-case tools.
- **Connects to:** New — but suggests Kompete should qualify prospects by product complexity or competitive market density

---

### Objection: Strategy Is Not Driven by Competitor Signals
- **Quote:** "We don't change our strategy basis competition looking at some signals from competition and we change our strategy."
- **Timestamp:** [19:55]
- **Severity:** Medium — challenges strategic use case; strategy ownership is founders + investors, not a team
- **Category:** Value Prop / GTM
- **Summary:** At Shobit's company, strategy is set by founders and investors in QBRs, not by a dedicated strategy function consuming external signals. This means the typical Kompete buyer persona (strategy, product marketing, or CI analyst) may not exist in companies of this size.
- **Connects to:** New — but reinforces the "30-member team has no dedicated CI function" pattern

---

### Objection: Large Enterprises Already Know Their Competition
- **Quote:** "Large enterprise may you know your competition very well. At least I was in the consumer industry."
- **Timestamp:** [27:04]
- **Severity:** Medium — raises question of value-add at the enterprise end of the spectrum
- **Category:** Market Fit
- **Summary:** Shobit suggests enterprise companies have enough internal resources or existing relationships to track competition informally. This creates a squeeze: startups won't pay, enterprises already know. The sweet spot he implicitly endorses is mid-market, which aligns with Kompete's target but narrows the narrative.
- **Connects to:** New — but structurally similar to the "we already do this manually" objection common in CI sales

---

### Objection: Messaging Intelligence Not Useful to Them

- **Severity:** Low — specific to this company's profile, not a broad objection

## Pain Points Validated

### Pain: Manual, Infrequent, and Resource-Heavy Competitive Benchmarking
- **Quote:** "After I think the first six months we did a competition benchmarking went to about six, seven competitors and look at their rates. Some of them were in the market, some of them we knew our clients who were using their services... I think we knew somebody who was working in that company."
- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Category:** Manual Process / Stale Data
- **Currently solving with:** Manual research via personal networks, client references, and informal industry contacts; done once and rarely revisited

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: The Real Buyer Is "Mid-Market With a Dedicated Competitive or Strategy Function"
- **Quote:** "Startups tend to believe that they their product is the best... they wouldn't spend money to get something like this. Mid market and beyond."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Kompete should qualify prospects not just by ARR but by whether they have a dedicated product marketing, CI, or strategy hire — the absence of such a function (as at Shobit's 30-person company) is a disqualifier regardless of company size.

---

### Insight: B2B Pricing Intelligence May Be a Feature, Not a Wedge
- **Quote:** "B2B pricing is always variable and dynamic... signals I'm not sure because we also did something recently. But absolutely useless."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Using pricing intelligence as the entry-point narrative may create skepticism before the broader platform value is demonstrated. Consider leading with hiring signals, G2 sentiment, or messaging shifts — all more tractable from public data — and positioning pricing as one layer within a richer signal stack.

---

### Insight: Fractional CROs and Strategy Consultants Are an Underexplored Distribution Channel
- **Quote:** "I'm also getting into a fractional CRO and pricing consultant role... I think it's really helpful for startups because the challenge startups is revenue and sales."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Fractional operators (CROs, CMOs, strategy advisors) working across multiple startups simultaneously could be a high-leverage distribution channel — they feel the competitive blind spot at scale and could become power users or resellers of Kompete's output layer.

---

### Insight: Informal Networks Are the Primary CI Mechanism at This Stage
- **Quote:** "Some of them we knew our clients who were using their services... I think we knew somebody who was working in that company."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The current alternative to Kompete is not Klue or Crayon — it's a founder's Rolodex. Kompete must position against "I'll just call someone who knows" as much as against legacy tools.

---

### Insight: Pricing Methodology Changes Only Happen Under Structural Competitive Pressure
- **Quote:** "It's only when you see a competition then you completely change your pricing methodology and say okay, hey this is my one time price and you don't worry about users. That's how we make those changes."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The trigger for engaging Kompete's pricing layer is a *competitor changing their pricing model* (e.g., seat → usage-based), not incremental price movements. Kompete should design alerts and case studies around these structural shifts as the high-urgency moment.
