# Interview Extract: Jeevithan Ramesh

**Date:** 20th February, 2026
**Role/Company:** Product Marketer at Inkle (B2B SaaS, US-focused bookkeeping/accounting platform); 4.5 years prior product marketing experience at another SaaS company. *Note: Jeevithan disclosed at the end of the call that he had resigned from Inkle that same day and had accepted an offer at a company in Whitefield.*
**Background:** Product marketer with ~4.5+ years of experience spanning positioning, battle cards, SEO/GEO, sales enablement, and competitive analysis. Currently at Inkle, a US-focused bookkeeping SaaS, where he owns product marketing end-to-end including competitor tracking, content, and website.

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: Pricing Intelligence Has Narrow Buyer Fit in Non-PLG Companies
- **Quote:** "The pricing part was actually really great. I didn't think about it because usually the pricing is decided by the head of GTM. It doesn't fully come to product marketing unless say it's a PLG company... In other companies, SLG or service-based company, that's mostly to do with customer success or product, whoever is senior there."
- **Timestamp:** [37:00]
- **Severity:** High — directly limits the pricing intelligence feature's relevance to a major segment of the target buyer persona (product marketers at SLG companies)
- **Category:** Market Fit
- **Summary:** Jeevithan validated that the pricing comparison UI looked impressive, but immediately noted that pricing decisions sit with GTM leads, not product marketers, at most non-PLG companies. Product marketers are "looped in" only to wordsmith the pricing page, not to set strategy. This constrains who can champion or buy a pricing-focused competitive product inside an organization.
- **Connects to:** New — but worth cross-referencing with any interviews involving GTM leads, Heads of Revenue, or PLG-oriented PMs who may be the true pricing intelligence buyer.

---

### Objection: Beta Features Are a Blind Spot — Automation Can't Fully Solve It
- **Quote:** "Assuming they don't just show it internally and do beta testing with their customers, then I don't think we would ever come to know. It's only when it's out in the public we would probably..."
- **Timestamp:** [25:12]
- **Severity:** Medium — caps the perceived completeness of feature-launch tracking; users will self-limit expectations
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** Jeevithan flagged that the most strategically relevant competitor developments — features in private beta — are inherently invisible to any monitoring tool. While he still values public launch tracking, this creates a ceiling on the perceived reliability of feature intelligence. Users may discount alerts if they know private betas are a gap.
- **Connects to:** New — but related to the broader "stale or incomplete data" trust issue common in competitive intel tools.

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: Product Marketers Are Users, Not Buyers, for Pricing Intel at SLG Companies
- **Quote:** "Product marketers are also looped into it just to see how we could maybe phrase it or how we could talk about it in public platforms or how the website pricing page could look like. But the pricing itself is not done by us."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The pricing intelligence module needs a separate buyer narrative targeting GTM leaders, Heads of Product, or Sales leadership — not product marketers. If sold through product marketing teams at SLG companies, pricing features may not drive purchase decisions even if the prospect finds them impressive.

---

### Insight: Sales Calls Are the Primary Competitive Intelligence Trigger — Not Scheduled Reviews
- **Quote:** "If the sales person is actually talking on a call and he comes and tells me that, hey, this particular prospect mentioned this company twice or thrice, then we would want to dig more deep into the company."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The demand for competitive intel is heavily event-driven (sales mentions a rival) rather than scheduled. Kompete's alerts and real-time feed directly address this trigger moment — this should be front and center in GTM messaging to product marketers.

---

### Insight: LLMs Pull Primarily from Reddit and LinkedIn — GEO Is an Emerging Priority
- **Quote:** "Right now the LLMs are taking most of the data from LinkedIn and from Reddit. Google is probably third or fourth or fifth... So they were just asking us to optimize Reddit more because many people look at Reddit for any form of analysis or research."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Kompete's AEO/GEO tracking feature has a strong near-term hook with product marketers who are already being told by founders to optimize for LLM visibility. This should be a prominent feature in pitches to marketing teams, not buried.

---

### Insight: Battle Card Ownership Varies by Company Stage and GTM Motion
- **Quote:** "In Inkle, not much, because we do not really — the sales enablement part is very less over here. But in my previous company that was something that product marketers used to own. So yeah, I've created a lot of battle cards in the last four and a half years."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Battle card automation is a stronger value prop for product marketers at companies with mature sales teams (Series B+, SLG motion). At earlier-stage or PLG companies, the sales enablement need is lower and this feature may not resonate as a core selling point.

---

### Insight: B2B Email Campaign Intelligence Is Desired But Assumed Impossible
- **Quote:** "We thought it's all private and no one could ever [get it]... That definitely it will be really helpful. But we never thought of it."
- **Implication for Kompete:** There is latent demand for competitor email/campaign monitoring that is currently suppressed because users assume it's technically infeasible. If Kompete can deliver even partial visibility (e.g., via newsletter subscription networks or honeypot accounts), it could be a genuine surprise differentiator — but it needs proof of concept before prospects will factor it into a purchase decision.

---

### Insight: Competitive Intel Tools Must Handle Up to 8–10 Competitors, Including Cross-Geography
- **Quote:** "We track, I think around 8 to 10. It's a mix of everything... they should be catering to the US market, so they could be incorporated anywhere."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Pricing tiers and UX should be built around multi-competitor tracking (8–10 is the norm, not an edge case). Geography-aware monitoring (tracking non-US companies that compete in the US market) is a baseline requirement, not a premium add-on.

## Persona Signals

- **Buyer or User?** Primarily a **User**. Jeevithan is an individual contributor product marketer. Pricing decisions sit with GTM leadership or product + sales collectively. He could become a champion who influences a purchase, but is unlikely to be the budget holder at most companies. At a PLG-first company, his influence over pricing and tooling would be higher.
- **Willingness to pay:** Not directly discussed. Strongly validated pain points suggest genuine willingness, but no budget signal surfaced. He is also transitioning companies, so his current budget context is moot.
- **Decision process:** Responds well to visual prototypes and evidence-backed recommendations (positive live reaction to Aditya's screen share). Would likely want a trial or pilot. Peer recommendations and direct product demos seem to be his evaluation mode.
- **Current tools mentioned:** Google Alerts (abandoned), Clay (heard of, untested), Semrush (previous company), Swiftly/Sifly (GEO/LLM ranking tool, current), ChatGPT, Perplexity, G2, Gartner, Trustpilot, Reddit, LinkedIn, HubSpot (competitor's CRM stack inference).
