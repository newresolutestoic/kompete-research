# Interview Extract: Sayantan Choudhury

**Date:** 2nd February, 2026
**Role/Company:** Engagement Manager, Pricing & Sales Strategy — Deloitte USI
**Background:** 10-year pricing veteran with stints at DHL Express (Country Pricing) and FedEx Express (Global Pricing Center of Excellence, founding hire). Now serves large US pharma clients at Deloitte, advising on end-to-end pricing strategy, go-to-market, and launch strategy.

---

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: Big 4 Consulting Firms Are Already Ahead on AI

- **Quote:** "Consulting firms, I would… can tell you are ahead of the curve. Way ahead of the curve… Deloitte partners with Nvidia… the AI solutions that you're talking about, I think we as a firm are doing a lot of stuff. And the problems that you are highlighting, I think some or the other team might be already working on."
- **Timestamp:** [21:00]
- **Severity:** Critical — directly challenges the consulting firm GTM persona
- **Category:** Market Fit
- **Summary:** Sayantan explicitly warns that large consulting firms like Deloitte are deeply embedded with AI partners (Nvidia, Microsoft, etc.) and likely already have internal initiatives covering the problem space Kompete is targeting. Selling into Big 4 may mean competing against their own internal buildouts, not just incumbent vendors. This effectively disqualifies big consulting firms as a near-term ICP.
- **Connects to:** New — specific to enterprise/consulting GTM track

---

### Objection: Strategy Work Is Fundamentally Hard to Automate

- **Quote:** "I wouldn't say that there's a process that you can actually really automate. Automate in the sense that maybe you can create a solution to the numeric or financial part… But then again, the thing with any exercise, right, with an AI agent or all those things, is that business is very dynamic."
- **Timestamp:** [17:34]
- **Severity:** High — challenges the automation/efficiency value prop for consulting workflows
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** From a consulting insider's perspective, strategy engagements rely heavily on judgment, client debate, and dynamic inputs (e.g., policy changes, clinical data shifts) that make full automation impractical. While he concedes financial/numeric models could be automated, the higher-value strategic layer remains human. This is a meaningful limitation if Kompete targets consulting workflow automation as a value driver.
- **Connects to:** New — relevant to any "automate analyst work" messaging

---

### Objection: Web Scraping Has Real Legal and Technical Limits

- **Quote:** "You have to work under the confines of privacy and security requirements for each and every website and each and every organization. Some websites, they will not even allow your web scraping bot to access… They will just block you."
- **Timestamp:** [10:46]
- **Severity:** Medium — a known technical challenge, but he validates it as a real barrier
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** Sayantan raises the practical reality that many enterprise and pharma websites actively block scrapers, and compliance constraints around data collection vary by industry. While he acknowledges web scraping is "one of the approaches," he flags it as unreliable. For Kompete, this reinforces the need for multi-source intelligence (not just web scraping) and clear data provenance.
- **Connects to:** New — technical risk signal specific to data sourcing

---

### Objection: Indian Market Is Too Price-Sensitive to Be a Strong Early Target

- **Quote:** "Indians are basically lazy and cheap… Indian firms would rather have you work for free for a few months and then tell you that hey, thank you very much, we don't need you."
- **Timestamp:** [27:36 / 22:13]
- **Severity:** Medium — not a product objection but a GTM risk for India-first strategy
- **Category:** GTM
- **Summary:** Sayantan draws on direct client experience (DHL India customers, logistics sector) to argue that Indian firms are highly price-sensitive and difficult to monetize early. He recommends targeting US/global firms as primary buyers. This aligns with Aditya's own hypothesis but adds conviction from an insider who has experienced this friction firsthand.
- **Connects to:** New — India GTM risk signal

---

### Objection: B2B Pricing Changes Are Annual — Reducing Urgency of Real-Time Alerts

- **Quote:** "B2B contracts, there has to be certainty in the windows contract. So they are mostly annual and you go to the customers annually. Even if you do benchmarking for a B2B contract, that is always annual."
- **Timestamp:** [09:30]
- **Severity:** Medium — partially challenges the "sub-3-minute alert" urgency angle for B2B pricing
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** In traditional B2B industries (logistics, pharma, manufacturing), pricing benchmarking happens on annual cycles. This means real-time pricing alerts may have limited urgency for buyers in these sectors. However, this objection likely does not apply to PLG SaaS companies where pricing pages change frequently — Kompete's primary ICP. Worth noting as a segmentation consideration.
- **Connects to:** New — relevant for non-SaaS segments

## Pain Points Validated

### Pain: Stale Spreadsheet Data With No Alerts

- **Quote:** "It's always probably in a spreadsheet that we maintain. Right. We try to put in the data say today after 15 days. If somebody changes something, it is not something that is reflected back on the spreadsheet. Still the old one."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** Stale Data
- **Currently solving with:** Manual Excel spreadsheets updated ad-hoc; no automated refresh or alert mechanism

---

### Pain: No Alerts When Competitors Change Messaging or Run Campaigns

- **Quote:** "There's no alert or any notification or say a competitor changes their positioning, they change their messaging or they're doing any campaigns… all those things were something that was probably not very out loud unless someone from sales team or customer success team, they go and interact with the people."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** Late Discovery
- **Currently solving with:** Word-of-mouth from sales/CS teams; no systematic monitoring in place

---

### Pain: Competitive Benchmarking Is Manual and Point-in-Time

- **Quote:** "That used to be all Excel etc… There's no iteration, there's no proper kind of say structure around that."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** Manual Process
- **Currently solving with:** Excel-based competitive grids built at a point in time, not maintained continuously

---

### Pain: Industry Teams Are Always Firefighting — No Time for Strategy

- **Quote:** "You're always firefighting all the time. You do not have time to like sit and think strategy. So that's where consultants come."
- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Category:** Manual Process
- **Currently solving with:** Hiring external consultants (Deloitte, Accenture) to do strategy work that internal teams don't have bandwidth for

---

### Pain: Pricing Is Under-Resourced and Under-Structured in SaaS

- **Quote:** "Pricing is a little under-looked in at least in the SaaS industry… it's more like intuitive and it's time based. It's done on a particular day and then there's no iteration, there's no proper kind of say structure around that."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** No System of Record
- **Currently solving with:** Intuition-driven, one-time pricing exercises; no ongoing pricing discipline or tooling

## Product Feedback & Feature Requests

### Feedback: Build a Plug-and-Play Modular Entry Product

- **Quote:** "You can create a plug and play solution… create a solution which is like generic and which then you can customize… it's like a foot in the door… a trial period for 30 days and then you tell them that see sir, you get price alerts from all these competitors. Like very basic solutions."
- **Type:** Positioning Feedback / Feature Request
- **Priority signal:** Strongly expressed — he returned to this multiple times, framed it as a strategic GTM recommendation, not just a product idea. "Would commercialize very quick."

---

### Feedback: Deal-Level Competitive Intelligence Module

- **Quote:** "Maybe a deal pre-deal module you can create where you can get competitive intelligence, product type, number, description of the competition etc. using AI… and then just tell them that sir, this is the competitive intelligence module that we have. We also have many other modules that you can buy from."
- **Type:** Feature Request
- **Priority signal:** Moderate — offered as a concrete use case anchor (Harsha Engineers in Ahmedabad) for manufacturing/B2B companies competing on parts pricing

---

### Feedback: Policy Change Tracking Could Be High Value for Regulated Industries

- **Quote:** "Suddenly a policy comes up, policy change happens. Then how will you — maybe that AI agent can then take into account, okay, these are the policy changes and then update the model."
- **Type:** Feature Request
- **Priority signal:** Speculative / exploratory — he framed it as something that "comes to mind" rather than a burning need. Could be a niche vertical expansion signal for pharma/regulated industries.

---

### Feedback: Multi-Stakeholder Team Delivery (Finance, Marketing, C-Suite)

- **Quote:** "Marketing, finance, commercialization. That strategy and Excel to the Excel strategy… You're talking to the C suite now. C suite code strategy. Then they'll use that information, take their own decisions."
- **Type:** Positioning Feedback
- **Priority signal:** Implicit — he describes how intelligence flows to different stakeholders (C-suite for strategy, finance for modeling, marketing for GTM). Aligns with Kompete's team-specific delivery differentiator.

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: Don't Target Big 4 — Target AI-Unaware Mid-Market US Firms

- **Quote:** "I would suggest to maybe target those firms which are new in the game who do not understand AI, who are looking to explore AI… There are many firms in the U.S. who don't know about AI and who are looking at different companies who can maybe start their journey and you can partner with them in their AI journey and handhold them."
- **Implication for Kompete:** This is a strong ICP signal from a Big 4 insider. The sweet spot may be $5–50M ARR B2B SaaS companies without dedicated CI/AI teams — not consulting firms who are building internally. Kompete should position as the AI-native competitive intelligence layer for companies who *haven't* yet invested in a Klue/Crayon contract or in-house CI capability.

---

### Insight: Annual B2B Pricing Cycles Create a Natural Upsell Wedge

- **Quote:** "Even if you do benchmarking for a B2B contract, that is always annual. B2C contracts work very differently."
- **Implication for Kompete:** The "annual benchmarking" pattern in B2B creates a natural entry point — Kompete can replace the ad-hoc, once-a-year spreadsheet exercise with a living system of record. The pitch isn't "real-time alerts" for these buyers; it's "your annual benchmarking, now always current."

---

### Insight: The Real Buyer Is Someone Tired of Paying Consultants for Competitive Research

- **Quote:** "You're always firefighting all the time. You do not have time to like sit and think strategy. So that's where consultants come… whatever people might say about consultants but we are useful at the end of the day."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Sayantan inadvertently positions Kompete as a partial consultant replacement for competitive intelligence work — giving in-house strategy and product teams the data layer that currently requires external engagement. The framing "get the intelligence without hiring a consultant" could resonate with CFOs and VPs of Strategy at mid-market companies.

---

### Insight: Competitive Intelligence for Physical Goods / Manufacturing Is Underserved

- **Quote:** "A fabrication company in Gujarat — Harsha Engineers — they manufacture all these ball bearings and steel fabricated parts for companies in Germany, Italy, US and there they have a lot of competitors because parts… you can get the parts intelligence information from any of the public other companies in China."
- **Implication for Kompete:** While Kompete is focused on B2B SaaS, Sayantan's example reveals an adjacent market — manufacturing/trading companies competing on parts pricing globally — where CI tooling is virtually nonexistent. Not an immediate priority, but a future vertical expansion signal.

## Persona Signals

- **Buyer or User?** Neither directly — Sayantan is an **influencer/advisor persona**. As an Engagement Manager at Deloitte, he advises clients but doesn't control a software budget. His firm (Deloitte) is explicitly flagged as a poor target due to internal AI buildouts. However, his clients (pharma, logistics, manufacturing companies) could be buyers.
- **Willingness to pay:** Not personally a buyer. He was candid that Indian firms are price-resistant and that US/global mid-market firms are better paymasters. No explicit personal WTP signal given.
- **Decision process:** Would evaluate based on ease of integration ("plug and play"), quick time-to-value (30-day trial), and modular pricing. He suggested a freemium/trial-first GTM approach as the right entry motion.
- **Current tools mentioned:** Excel / spreadsheets (manual competitive benchmarking), web scraping (mentioned as an approach, not a current tool he uses), focus groups / expert interviews (for pharma pricing research)
