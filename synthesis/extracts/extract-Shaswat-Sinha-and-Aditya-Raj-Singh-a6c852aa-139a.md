# Interview Extract: Shaswat Sinha

**Date:** 10th February, 2026
**Role/Company:** Former Product Manager, Bluehost (Shared Hosting Vertical); currently founding a B2B SaaS tool for small-scale manufacturing businesses in Jamshedpur
**Background:** Shaswat is an experienced Product Manager who led the shared hosting vertical at Bluehost, growing and restructuring pricing tiers with measurable revenue impact. He recently left to build his own startup and has direct, hands-on experience doing competitive analysis manually against Hostinger and GoDaddy.

---

## Objections & Challenges Raised

### Objection: Real-Time Alerts Are Low Priority for PMs
- **Quote:** "If as a PM real time monitoring was something that I… I would say two and a half [out of 5]. I don't think that would be useful. But not so much useful that I would buy the product for your stuff."
- **Timestamp:** [23:24] – [25:10]
- **Severity:** High — directly challenges the sub-3-minute alert value prop
- **Category:** Value Prop
- **Summary:** Shaswat explicitly rates real-time competitive monitoring as a 2.5/5 for his PM use case. He argues that for product work, his team operated efficiently using time-zone differences and pre-set metrics, making live alerting feel redundant. He frames real-time monitoring as mainly useful for DevOps and on-call engineers, not PMs or marketers.
- **Connects to:** New — but relates to the broader "who actually needs alerts" buyer persona question

---

### Objection: ChatGPT Already Gets Me Halfway There
- **Quote:** "As a product manager market research before AI after a market research. I'm already halfway through. But this software if I start using this I feel that instead of starting from 5 on 10 I will start with a 7.5 on 10… GPT gives me a set of options I have to figure out."
- **Timestamp:** [46:52]
- **Severity:** High — signals that AI-native users already have a functional (if imperfect) free alternative
- **Category:** Differentiation
- **Summary:** Shaswat positions ChatGPT as already solving ~50% of his competitive research needs. Kompete's value is framed as a productivity multiplier (5→7.5 out of 10) rather than a fundamental capability unlock. This is meaningful: the perceived delta above ChatGPT is real but modest, and it anchors willingness to pay lower. He acknowledges Kompete would be better but doesn't express urgency.
- **Connects to:** ChatGPT/Perplexity comparison — this is a recurring objection pattern for AI-powered research tools

---

### Objection: Internal Data Beats Competitor Data for PM Decisions
- **Quote:** "This would be more result driven for you. Because as a product manager I can't only give a hypothesis this will work because they worked for you. But this is internal data that we're talking about… fixing those problems from the direction is very concrete when you have the relevant internal data for it."
- **Timestamp:** [28:44] – [29:12]
- **Severity:** Medium — challenges whether PMs are primary buyers vs. users
- **Category:** Market Fit
- **Summary:** Shaswat argues that PMs drive decisions primarily from internal customer data (support tickets, analytics, journey mapping), not competitive signals. External competitive intelligence is supplementary rather than directional. This questions whether the PM persona is a buyer or just an occasional user of competitive intel.
- **Connects to:** Buyer persona confusion — raises the question of whether sales/marketing teams are a stronger primary buyer than PMs

---

### Objection: A Simple AI Agent Could Do the Basic Version
- **Quote:** "Now I think that we can do a simple AI agent who would just scrape this one particular website once a day and give you the changes that would be very much easy now."
- **Timestamp:** [20:07]
- **Severity:** Medium — challenges the moat/differentiation of the core monitoring feature
- **Category:** Differentiation
- **Summary:** When Aditya demonstrated competitor pricing page monitoring, Shaswat immediately suggested it could be replicated with a simple scraping agent. While he validates the use case, he frames the basic version as "easy to build," which could affect perceived pricing power. This objection implicitly asks: what's the hard part that justifies the price?
- **Connects to:** New — but relates to the "build vs. buy" objection common in early sales

---

### Objection: Platform Feels Like Passive Information Delivery
- **Quote:** "Is there anything else that the user will be able to perform on your platform or it's just they will be receiving information based on the data that they have fed into the system?"
- **Timestamp:** [11:39]
- **Severity:** Medium — signals concern about actionability and engagement
- **Category:** Product
- **Summary:** Shaswat probes whether the platform is purely a monitoring/reporting dashboard or enables action. When told "that will come later," he seems to accept it but the question reveals an expectation gap — users want to do something with the intelligence, not just receive it. This is consistent with his own product instincts (he built upselling flows, not just dashboards).
- **Connects to:** New — actionability gap; relates to the battle card / strategic recommendations feature that needs clearer emphasis

## Pain Points Validated

### Pain: Competitor Pricing Changes Go Undetected
- **Quote:** "No [we don't track that]. Now I think that we can do a simple AI agent who would just scrape this one particular website once a day and give you the changes."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** Stale Data
- **Currently solving with:** Not tracked at all; discovered organically or via manual periodic reviews

---

### Pain: Competitive Intelligence Siloed Across Teams
- **Quote:** "The customer success team was only for [reviews]. So if any major issue or bug used to happen, we used to get notified and take the steps accordingly."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** Tool Fragmentation
- **Currently solving with:** Each team (CS, product, marketing) operates independently; no unified competitive view

---

### Pain: Manual Competitive Research Is Time-Consuming but Impactful
- **Quote:** "I did some competitive research, I found out that GoDaddy Hostinger, they were selling at least seven to eight plans on the shared hosting profile itself… I implemented the similar format on bluehost. At least 22 to 25% of the clients who were on the basic moved to the premium plan."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Category:** Manual Process
- **Currently solving with:** Manual browsing of competitor pricing pages; no systematic cadence; ad hoc when doing major product initiatives

---

### Pain: Review Monitoring Not Systematic
- **Quote:** "We don't track it. So basically at bluehost we had a customer success team that was only for this."
- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Category:** Manual Process / Stale Data

---

### Pain: Late Discovery of Competitor Feature/Product Launches

- **Category:** Late Discovery

## Strategic Insights

### Insight: Competitive Intelligence ROI Is Easiest to Prove Through Pricing Impact
- **Quote:** "At least 22 to 25% of the clients who were on the basic moved to the premium plan. So that was a chunk of revenue that straight away got added."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Pricing tier competitive intelligence has the clearest, most quantifiable ROI story. This should anchor the pitch — not "monitor your competitors" but "our pricing intelligence drove a 22-25% plan upgrade rate for customers like Bluehost." ROI calculators built around pricing optimization will resonate strongly with PM and monetization-focused buyers.

---

### Insight: PMs Value Competitor Intelligence as a Starting Multiplier, Not a Decision Engine
- **Quote:** "Instead of starting from 5 on 10 I will start with a 7.5 on 10. I will be very much sure about the metric that I want to focus on."
- **Implication for Kompete:** PMs won't buy Kompete because they can't do competitive analysis without it — they'll buy it because it compresses research time and increases confidence. The pitch to PMs should be "save 40% of your research sprint time" rather than "replace your competitive research process."

---

### Insight: The Real Buyer Is the Team That Loses Deals, Not the Team That Builds Product
- **Quote:** "I would still look out for more teams to be helped out of this. And that sale is something that my product can take care of."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Shaswat's instinct is that sales teams have a higher pain threshold and therefore a higher willingness to pay. He's essentially suggesting a land-and-expand motion: start with marketing/product, but the sales team angle (battle cards, objection handling, win/loss) is what unlocks "must have" status and a higher price point.

---

### Insight: Geography-Specific Competitive Differentiation Is a Real But Underserved Use Case
- **Quote:** "Do you also check which features are probably the differentiators in a particular geography? … When you want to tackle someone India, hashtags and taglines that Zomato use works very amazingly good. But when you move to the MENA region… you have to be very strict and concrete and very professional."
- **Implication for Kompete:** B2B SaaS companies with multi-region GTM need geo-segmented competitive intelligence — not just global feature comparisons but regional positioning, messaging tone, and pricing localization. This is a potential differentiated product angle not visible in the current feature set.

---

### Insight: "Public IQ" Referenced as a Competitor or Adjacent Tool
- **Quote:** "Last one the public IQ that was the company's name… Free open source assessment."
- **Implication for Kompete:** Shaswat mentioned "Public IQ" (set up in Bangalore, someone he knows personally) in the context of competitive tools. This warrants a competitive landscape scan — it may be a direct or adjacent competitor in the Indian market that is not on Kompete's current radar.

## Persona Signals

- **Buyer or User?** Primarily a **User** in his Bluehost context (would use the tool; budget authority unclear); potential **Buyer** in his current startup context (small scale, cost-sensitive)
- **Willingness to pay:** Moderate-to-low. He rates the product 7.5-8/10 and frames it as "good to have." His current startup context (building a lean CRM for small factories) suggests high pricing sensitivity. He acknowledged PLG/low-cost entry pricing would be appropriate and noted competitors like Klue/Crayon are too expensive for his context.
- **Decision process:** Data-driven; would want to see clear ROI before purchase. Would evaluate based on: (1) which teams it serves, (2) whether intelligence is actionable vs. passive, (3) whether it improves on ChatGPT meaningfully. No formal procurement process in current startup mode — likely a direct founder decision.
- **Current tools mentioned:** Google Analytics, Tableau, AW Stats, internal CS team ticketing, manual competitor website browsing, ChatGPT (for market research), unnamed agentic workflow tool (at Bluehost), Salesforce (mentioned as too expensive at 25-30K for SMBs)
