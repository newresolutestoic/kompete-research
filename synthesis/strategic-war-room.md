## Mission & Vision

**Mission:** Make competitive intelligence autonomous, actionable, and accessible to every revenue team — not just the companies that can afford $50K+/yr tools or dedicated CI headcount.

**Vision:** Become the competitive operating system for B2B companies — the single place where every competitor move is captured, every pattern is recognized, and every team knows exactly what to do about it.

**Why now:** AI has reached the point where the entire CI workflow — monitoring, analysis, synthesis, and recommendation — can be automated. Legacy CI tools (Klue, Crayon) were built in the pre-AI era. Perplexity/ChatGPT are general-purpose and have no memory, no monitoring, no system of record. The gap is wide open.

## Marketing Principles Framework (Godin)

**Full applied framework:** [`seth-godin-marketing-principles.md`](../../seth-godin-marketing-principles.md) in the website repo.

Synthesized across Godin's books (*Purple Cow*, *Tribes*, *Permission Marketing*, *This Is Marketing*, *All Marketers Are Liars*, *Ideavirus*, *The Practice / Dip / Linchpin*) plus April 2026 blog posts. The 7 principles form a MECE lens we use to test every marketing decision.

### The 7 principles, applied one-line

| # | Principle | Kompete.ai application |
|---|-----------|------------------------|
| 1 | **Purpose** — the change you seek to make | "Stop losing deals to competitor moves you never saw." Not "AI-native CI platform." |
| 2 | **People** — smallest viable audience | Founders & CMOs at $5–50M ARR B2B SaaS who personally spend 10 hrs/week on competitor research. Serving 6 personas on the hero = serving 0. |
| 3 | **Product** — remarkable (Purple Cow) | Lead with one Purple Cow, not a feature tour. Our four: AEO, Claim Verification, Strategy Timeline, Sub-3-min alerts. |
| 4 | **Permission** — anticipated, personal, relevant | Weekly public CI newsletter is the highest-leverage permission asset we're not yet shipping. |
| 5 | **Promise** — true story matching a worldview | Lead with feeling ("never be caught off-guard again"), close with facts (Claim Verification, 3-min alerts). |
| 6 | **Propagation** — engineered to spread (Ideavirus) | "State of Competitive Intelligence 2026" report + auto-generated comparison pages = spread vehicles built into product. |
| 7 | **Persistence** — ship the practice, push through the Dip | Dated snapshots + weekly shipping cadence. Resist quitting during the 90–180 day evangelism Dip. |

### Top 7 marketing moves (ranked by Godin-leverage)

1. Pick *one* canonical hero story and lead the homepage with it (from `competitive-comparison-stories.md` — Story A or B).
2. Launch a weekly public "This week in B2B competitive moves" newsletter.
3. Name and claim the tribe: *"People like us do things like this."*
4. Ship a free, anonymized "State of Competitive Intelligence 2026" report from our 27 interviews.
5. Publish the founder origin story — "Why I built Kompete after 10 hrs/week on competitor research didn't work."
6. Build one signature "Free Prize Inside" — e.g., first screen shows a live scrape of the visitor's *own* competitors.
7. Identify the 30/20/10 sneezer list (PMMs, sales leaders, founders) and build 1:1 relationships.

### How this framework resolves existing Open Questions

- **OQ #1 (positioning language):** Godin's *Purpose* principle says pick the *change*, not the *category*. *"Competitive Revenue Engine"* or *"Competitive Operating System"* beats *"Competitive Intelligence Platform"* — the former names the change we seek to make, the latter describes a software category.
- **OQ #6 (outcome vs capability messaging):** Godin's *Promise* principle (reinforced by his April 2026 *"The right answer"* post — feelings have an evolutionary head start on facts) gives us the narrative structure. Hero should land a feeling in 5 seconds; proof-stack should land facts in the next 30.
- **Sanchit's persona-spread objection (Obj #5):** directly echoes Godin's *"smallest viable audience"* — confirms we should narrow the hero to founders/CMOs at $5–50M ARR and let the product tabs serve the other 4 personas.
- **Anti-AI-slop positioning** (from April 2026 *"Attention and effort"* post): in a world flooding with spammy AI outreach, Claim Verification becomes a trust-asset, not just a feature. Lean into this in brand voice — *Kompete is the anti-slop CI tool, even though it's AI-native.*

### The test we run before any marketing decision

Before shipping any headline, page, ad, or launch:
1. Can we answer **Purpose** and **People** in one sentence each? If not, stop and fix that first.
2. Is there ONE Purple Cow visible, not a feature buffet?
3. Does it feel *kinder than necessary* (Godin, Apr 6, 2026) — or just transactional?
4. If someone screenshots this, does it confer status to share? (Propagation test.)
5. Are we *creating the conditions for magic* (Godin, Apr 10, 2026) — or treating an important interaction as ordinary?

#### Objection 1: "Buyer vs. User Persona Confusion — Who owns the budget?"

**The challenge:** PMM is an "orphan" function — budget shifts between CPO, CEO, CBO. If you serve multiple functions (product, marketing, sales, CS), it's unclear who owns the budget and makes the buying decision.

**Our response (evolving):**

This is our most important GTM question. Here's our current thinking:

**Short answer:** We don't sell to PMM. We sell to the person who loses money when competitive intelligence fails. That's usually the **VP Marketing/CMO** (owns pipeline) or the **CEO/Founder** (at $5-50M ARR, they ARE the CI function).

**The land-and-expand play:**
1. **Land with Marketing or Founder.** PMM is the user, not the buyer. The buyer is whoever owns pipeline and win rate. At $5-50M ARR, that's often the CEO directly. At $50-200M, it's the CMO or VP Marketing.
2. **Expand to Sales.** Battle cards in CRM drive adoption with sales. Sales leadership sees win rate improvement. Budget shifts or adds from sales enablement.
3. **Expand to Product.** Product uses feature gap analysis and roadmap intelligence. Budget comes from product org.
4. **The hook:** The weekly strategic brief goes to the CEO regardless. Once the CEO sees it, it becomes a company tool, not a department tool.

**Why this works (evidence):**
- Klue and Crayon sell to PMM — and their #1 complaint is that nobody else in the org uses it. We solve distribution by design (team-specific briefs, Slack channels, CRM integration).
- At companies <$50M ARR, there IS no PMM. The CEO, a PM, or a growth marketer does CI ad hoc. We replace their manual process directly.
- Sankalp (HighRadius): "Competitor tables are our biggest MQL contributor" — this is a marketing ROI story, not a PMM budget story.

**Open question:** Do we lead with "competitive intelligence platform" (sounds like PMM tool) or "competitive revenue engine" (sounds like it belongs to RevOps/CMO)? Need to test positioning.

#### Objection 2: "ROI Justification is Hard for PMM"

**The challenge:** PMM is a cost function. Hard to calculate ROI. ClearTax example — raising ASP from 5L to 20-25L made ROI justification extremely difficult.

**Our response (evolving):**

**We don't sell ROI on CI. We sell ROI on outcomes CI drives.**

The mistake Klue/Crayon make: they justify their $40-80K/yr price by saying "CI is important." That's a losing argument because CI as a function has fuzzy ROI.

**Our ROI framing:**
| Outcome | Metric | Dollar Value |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Win rate improvement on competitive deals | Even 3-5% lift | At 100 enterprise deals/yr × $50K ACV × 5% = **$250K incremental revenue** |
| Churn prevention from competitive displacement | Save 2-3 accounts/quarter | 3 × $40K = **$120K ARR saved/quarter** |
| Pipeline from competitive content (comparison pages, battle cards) | MQLs from comparison pages | "Biggest contributor of MQLs" — Sankalp |
| Time saved — PMM/founder hours reclaimed | 15 hrs/week × $100/hr equivalent | **$78K/year in time** |

**The key insight:** We price at a fraction of legacy ($3-5K/yr vs $40-80K), so the ROI bar is dramatically lower. A single saved deal pays for 3+ years of Kompete.

**ClearTax comparison counter:** The higher the ASP, the MORE valuable CI becomes, not less. If a deal is worth $20-25L, losing one competitive deal because your battle card was stale is a $20-25L mistake. Our value scales with deal size.

**Analogy:** Slack doesn't build separate products for engineering, sales, and marketing. It's one product with channels. Kompete is one intelligence engine with team-specific delivery channels.

#### Objection 6: "Lock-in Problem — What Stops Someone Using Glean?"

**The challenge:** What creates lock-in? What stops a company from using Glean or another tool and feeding it competitive data?

**Our response (evolving):**

**Three layers of lock-in:**

1. **Strategy Timeline (time-based moat):** The longer you use Kompete, the more historical data you accumulate. After 12 months, you have a complete competitive history that no other tool can replicate. Glean can search today's documents — it can't give you "how has Competitor X's pricing evolved over the last 18 months?"

2. **Calibrated intelligence (learning moat):** Over time, Kompete learns what YOUR company cares about. Which signals matter to your deals. Which competitors are actually relevant. What "high severity" means for your context. A general-purpose tool starts from zero every time.

3. **Workflow lock-in:** Battle cards embedded in Salesforce. Alerts routing to team-specific Slack channels. Weekly CEO brief that's become part of the operating rhythm. Comparison pages auto-updating on your website. Ripping this out is like ripping out your CRM — technically possible, organizationally painful.

**On Glean specifically:** Glean is internal knowledge search. It searches what you already know. Kompete discovers what you don't know yet — competitive signals from external sources that aren't in any internal document. They're complementary, not competitive. (This is actually a partnership opportunity.)

**Deeper vs. transient problems (Sanchit's framing):**
- **Transient:** Hiring signals, one-off news — yes, these are point-in-time and low lock-in
- **Deep:** Strategy Timeline, calibrated alerts, workflow integration, historical pattern recognition — these compound over time and create genuine switching costs

#### Objection 8: "Signal vs. Noise Problem Remains Unsolved"

**The challenge:** AI gives surface-level answers without understanding company-specific context. Finding real signals in noisy competitive data is where human PMs are still valuable.

**Our response (evolving):**

**This is the hardest and most honest objection. We partially agree.**

**What AI can do today (and we do well):**
- Monitor at scale — no human can track 15 competitors across 8 dimensions daily
- Surface changes — detect that something happened (pricing changed, feature launched, review spike)
- Cross-reference — correlate a hiring surge with a product launch signal
- Structured analysis — feature matrices, pricing comparisons, review aggregation

**Where the gap is (and our plan to close it):**
- **Context-specific interpretation:** "Competitor X hired 12 ML engineers" means different things to different companies. We solve this by learning your context over time — your product, your market position, your deal patterns.
- **Strategic synthesis:** "What does this MEAN for us?" requires understanding your strategy. Our approach: the Strategy Timeline + company context builds this over time. The first week is generic. By month 3, recommendations are calibrated to your reality.
- **Action quality:** "Update battle card" is generic. "Update the pricing objection handler for the ACME deal closing next week because Competitor X just undercut by 20%" is specific. We get more specific as we integrate with CRM and learn deal context.

**The honest answer:**
> "Day 1, we're better than manual research but not better than a great PMM who knows the space deeply. By month 3, we're better than that PMM because we never forget, never miss a signal, and have pattern data they can't hold in their head. The PMM's job doesn't go away — it evolves from hunting to strategizing. We do the hunting. They do the thinking."

**Key interview evidence:**
- "Any agent can be built to scrap data and give a summary. The decision is far more important." — Rajesh Sahu. **This is exactly our roadmap.** Phase 1: data + summaries (now). Phase 2: decisions + actions (next 6 months). Phase 3: autonomous execution (12+ months — auto-update comparison pages, auto-generate counter-campaigns).
- "If you will build some insights from that data, it will just flip the whole game." — Garima (BrowserStack)

1. **Review Intelligence already bridges this gap.** G2/Capterra reviews from competitor customers ARE customer voice. "What are competitor customers complaining about?" = where the market opportunity is.
2. **Win/Loss signals** tie CI to actual deal outcomes — what competitor moves actually impacted your customers' decisions.
3. **The synthesis layer** should always answer "so what should we do for OUR customers?" not just "what did the competitor do?"

**Design implication:** Every alert and brief should include a "customer impact" line — not just "Competitor X did Y" but "This likely affects Z segment of your customers because..."

#### Objection 13: "Low Subscription, Usage-Heavy Pricing"

**The challenge:** "Keep the subscription cost low and usage price heavy. Users can't question you [on cost] because they used it."

**Key context:** At SaaS Labs/JustCall, they saw churn from pricing changes when subscription costs were high. When usage-based, customers accepted higher total spend because they felt in control.

**Our response (evolving):**

This is concrete pricing architecture advice. Current thinking:

| Model | Pros | Cons |
|-------|------|------|
| **Flat subscription ($3-5K/yr)** | Simple, predictable ARR | Hard to justify for small teams; feels expensive upfront |
| **Low sub + usage (Siddharth's suggestion)** | Low barrier to start, scales with value, users feel in control | Harder to forecast revenue; need clear usage meters |
| **Per-competitor pricing** | Directly ties cost to value received | Users may limit competitors tracked to save money (bad for us) |
| **Freemium + paid tiers** | Volume adoption, viral potential | Free users may never convert; support cost |

**Siddharth's model applied to Kompete:**
- **$0-$49/mo subscription** — gets you in the door: company profile, basic competitor landscape discovery, weekly summary email
- **Usage charges on:**
  - Number of competitors actively tracked (deep monitoring)
  - Number of team seats receiving briefs
  - Custom report generation
  - CRM/Slack integration active connections
  - API calls for programmatic access

**This aligns with the "2-3% of consulting cost" framing:** A $100K consulting engagement equivalent becomes $2-3K/yr with usage scaling. The entry price is negligible; the value-based usage is where revenue grows.

**Open question:** Does per-competitor pricing discourage broad tracking (bad for us) or create natural expansion revenue (good for us)?

#### Objection 15: "What Can You Capture That I Can't Get from a ChatGPT Prompt?"

#### Objection 16: "Static vs Dynamic Information — Only Dynamic Is Worth Paying For"

**The challenge:** Siddharth introduced the most useful framework from any interview. He divides competitive information into two buckets:

- **Static:** Feature parity, pricing pages, product offerings — "stale" info that doesn't update dynamically. Available to anyone. Low willingness to pay.
- **Dynamic:** Customer acquisitions by competitors, leadership departures, campaign launches, trigger events — time-sensitive, directly tied to business decisions. HIGH willingness to pay.

**Key quotes:**
- *"Static information is stale information which will not update dynamically over time. Dynamic information is what your buyer will be very keen [on]."*
- *"If your data can tell them 'this customer which is your target domain has been acquired by a competitor' — that's a business value. If it builds over time it gives me how many customers I have lost to competition."*
- *"Focus on dynamic, not static information."*

**Our response (evolving):**

This is a critical product design principle. It reframes everything.

**What counts as dynamic (= worth paying for):**
| Signal | Why It's Dynamic | Business Action |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| Competitor acquired your target customer | Immediate revenue loss signal | CS/Sales: win-back campaign or defend similar accounts |
| Competitor launched campaign targeting your base | Time-sensitive threat | Marketing: counter-campaign within 24hrs |
| Leadership departure at competitor | Window of opportunity | Sales: target their destabilized accounts |
| Pricing change | Deal-level impact | Sales: adjust pricing in active negotiations |
| Competitor product outage/crisis | Short-lived opportunity | Sales: outbound to frustrated customers NOW |
| Negative review surge | Sentiment shift in progress | Marketing: comparison content, SDR outreach |

**What counts as static (= nice-to-have, not worth paying for alone):**
| Signal | Why It's Static |
|--------|----------------|
| Feature comparison matrix | Changes quarterly at best |
| Competitor "about us" page | Rarely changes |
| Integration list | Updates slowly |
| Pricing page (enterprise SaaS) | Changes 1-2x/year |

**Product implication:** Our **Competitive Feed** (the scrolling ticker on v6.1/v6.2) is the dynamic layer — it should be the product's heartbeat. The static layers (feature matrix, competitor profiles) are the foundation, but the dynamic signals are what make people open the dashboard twice a day.

**Pricing implication:** Dynamic signals = premium tier. Static analysis = free/entry tier. This aligns perfectly with Siddharth's earlier advice on low-subscription + usage-heavy pricing.

#### Objection 17: "Will I Pay for It? Information vs Business Value"

**The challenge:** The most direct articulation of the ROI problem. Siddharth draws a hard line: information I'll happily READ for free. Information that helps me DO MORE BUSINESS, I'll pay for.

**Key quotes:**
- *"A competitive information which does not help me scale my business or get new business is something I would not put money upon. Happy to read it. It's a good read. I would love to read. But will I pay for it?"*
- *"I will be happy to pay when it helps me do business."*
- *"If it's an information that does not help me do more business, help me increase revenue — already analysts [are doing this]. If you are giving me something that helps me do more business, then I'm willing to pay you."*

**Our response (evolving):**

This is the **"so what?" test** that every piece of intelligence must pass. It connects to Sanchit's ROI objection (#2) but is more fundamental: it's not about calculating ROI — it's about whether the information is inherently tied to a revenue action.

**Framework for every alert/insight we surface:**

❌ **Information:** "Competitor X updated their homepage messaging."
✅ **Business value:** "Competitor X is now positioning against your core value prop. 3 active deals in your pipeline mention this exact feature. Here's the counter-narrative for sales."

❌ **Information:** "Competitor Y hired 8 ML engineers."
✅ **Business value:** "Competitor Y is building AI capabilities that match your Q3 roadmap. If they ship first, it affects $400K in pipeline where AI is a differentiator. Consider accelerating."

**Product rule:** Every notification must include a "business impact" line and a "recommended action." If we can't articulate the business impact, we shouldn't send the notification — it's noise.

#### Objection 18: "Sales Team Already Knows Faster Than Public Data"

**The challenge:** At enterprise scale (LeadSquared, 1500+ employees), field teams — sales, presales, marketing campaign teams — bring competitive intelligence faster than any public data source. "Before it will even come in public domain" — they know a competitor's leadership is leaving 2 months before LinkedIn.

**Key quotes:**
- *"If your sales team is active, you would know it immediately that something has come from the customer or competitor."*
- *"Even before LinkedIn is updated that a leadership position guy has left, we already know that the guy is leaving two months before. And that information is floating in the market. It is not in public domain."*

**Our response (evolving):**

Siddharth is right — for LARGE enterprises with 1000+ field employees. This actually helps us **sharpen our ICP:**

| Company Size | CI Source | Kompete's Value |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **1-50 employees** | Founder doing it manually | **Core replacement** — we ARE their CI function |
| **50-500 employees** | Ad hoc, sales anecdotes, no system | **System of record** — captures what sales mentions in Slack before it's lost |
| **500-2000 employees** | Analysts + sales pulse + some tools | **Augmentation** — fills gaps in geographic coverage, surfaces what sales doesn't see |
| **2000+ employees** | Dedicated CI team, field intelligence, consulting | **Lower value** — their field team is faster on many signals. Our value = automation + synthesis + historical tracking |

**Key insight:** Our sweet spot is $5-200M ARR (roughly 50-1500 employees) where:
- Sales team exists but isn't big enough to be a comprehensive intelligence network
- No dedicated CI analyst (or 1 overwhelmed PMM)
- Intel is scattered across Slack, email, people's heads — no system of record
- Geographic blind spots (e.g., Indian company selling in US doesn't have US field eyes)

**Siddharth's geography point validates a use case:** *"New geography... you're a smaller organization, you don't have field eyes and field ears to get you what you need there."* This is exactly where automated public-data monitoring beats human networks.

#### Objection 19: "Make Me Open Your Dashboard Twice a Day"

**The challenge:** The ultimate product quality bar. If the dynamic information is so valuable that users open the dashboard twice a day, the product wins. If it's a monthly read, it loses.

**Key quote:** *"If you bring that information which makes me open your dashboard like twice a day... that is very important for my business. And that will always be linked to business, not to information."*

**Our response (evolving):**

This is the **engagement metric** we should be optimizing for. Not DAU for vanity — DAU because the product delivers time-sensitive business value.

**What makes someone open twice a day:**
1. **Morning:** Weekly/daily brief arrived. "Here's what changed overnight across your competitors." (This is why the weekly digest was #5 feature demand at 12/27.)
2. **During the day:** Push alert. "Competitor just dropped pricing by 20%. 3 of your active prospects affected." (This is why sub-3-minute alerts was #1 feature demand at 20/27.)

**What does NOT make someone open twice a day:**
- Feature comparison matrices (updated quarterly)
- Competitor profiles (reference material)
- Historical strategy timeline (periodic deep-dives)

**Product architecture implication:** The feed/alert layer IS the product. Everything else (profiles, matrices, timeline) is the reference library. **The feed should be the default landing page** — not a competitor profile or dashboard overview.

**This validates our v6.1/v6.2 design choice:** The scrolling intel feed as the first thing people see on the landing page is exactly right — it demonstrates the dynamic nature of the product.

#### Insight 20: "Trigger Events Drive Deep Analysis, Not Continuous Monitoring"

**The challenge:** CI doesn't happen on a fixed schedule. It's trigger-driven. Monthly baseline checks + deep dives when something happens.

**Key quote:** *"There has to be some event for which you have to dig deep... Pulse daily from sales team. But large features, a competitor has launched — the pulse of it comes very quickly."*

**Pattern he described:**
1. **Baseline:** Monthly review of market landscape (what's the state of play?)
2. **Trigger event:** Sales reports competitor demo'd something new / customer mentions competitor / marketing sees campaign
3. **Deep dive:** Activated by trigger — "what is the impact of this offering?"
4. **Update:** Existing competitive database updated with new intel

**Product implication:** Our system should support BOTH modes:
- **Continuous monitoring** (what Kompete does automatically) → surfaces the trigger events
- **On-demand deep dive** (what the user does when a trigger fires) → competitor profiles, feature matrices, strategy timeline

**The magic:** We turn mode 1 into an automated, always-on layer. The human only needs to engage for mode 2 — and we make mode 2 faster by having all the data pre-assembled.

## Open Questions

These are unresolved strategic questions that need further discussion and testing:

1. **Positioning language:** "Competitive Intelligence Platform" vs "Competitive Revenue Engine" vs "Competitive Operating System" — which resonates with buyers (not users)?
2. **Hero feature for launch:** Strategy Timeline (unique moat) vs AEO (unique + trendy) vs Auto-Battle Cards (most immediate pain) vs **Competitor Discovery (aha moment per Siddharth)** — which do we lead with?
3. **Pricing model:** Siddharth strongly advocates low-subscription + usage-based. Sanchit flags ROI justification difficulty. **Test: $0-49/mo base + per-competitor + per-seat usage.** Does per-competitor pricing discourage or encourage expansion?
4. **Consulting angle:** Do we pursue Big 4/boutique consulting firms as a beachhead (Rashi at KPMG validated demand) or stay focused on direct B2B SaaS?
5. **India vs US-first:** Current plan is US-first with India design partners. Is there a faster path to revenue in India for companies like Anaplan, HighRadius, HackerRank?
6. **Outcome vs capability messaging:** Siddharth (and frankly, all good marketing practice) says lead with outcomes. **Action: audit all website copy and reframe.** Current hero sections are feature-led.
7. **Competitor Discovery in signup flow:** Should this be the first thing users see (instant value, "aha moment") or a premium feature? Siddharth's suggestion implies it should be the hook.
8. **Customer context integration:** How do we ensure CI recommendations are always tied back to "what this means for YOUR customers" (Siddharth's pivot from competitor-watching to customer-listening)?
9. **Static vs Dynamic product tiers:** Should static analysis (feature matrix, profiles) be the free tier and dynamic signals (alerts, feed, trigger events) be the paid tier? Siddharth's framework strongly suggests this split.
10. **"Twice a day" engagement bar:** What specific dynamic signals would make our ICP open the product twice daily? Need to identify and prioritize the top 5 trigger events by business impact.
11. **The ChatGPT objection is THE #1 pitch obstacle:** Two independent operators asked it unprompted. Must be addressed proactively on website, in sales deck, and in the first 30 seconds of every demo. Current answer needs to be compressed to a 10-second soundbite.
12. **Enterprise ceiling:** At 2000+ employees, field teams are faster than public data. Is this a hard ceiling on our TAM or can we add value through synthesis + historical tracking even for large enterprises?
